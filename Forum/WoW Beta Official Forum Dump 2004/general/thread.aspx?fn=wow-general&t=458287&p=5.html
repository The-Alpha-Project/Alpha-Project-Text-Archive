 88. Re: Mask classes in PvP,and group levels by c | 10/20/2004 8:21:53 PM GMTDT
I really like this idea. The game gives you much more information than your character would have, which kinda forces artificial imbalances in PvP.

IRL, I can look at a jackbooted thug wielding a baseball bat, and I know that I probably don't want to mess with him (red con, as it were), but I don't know how much he can bench (level).

If I were in the WoW world, I'd be able to look at someone else and tell at a glance their race, and maybe some hint of their role (cloth wearer, probably a caster. Plate...must be a warrior or paladin.), but I wouldn't be able to tell exactly what they were unless they were using some class-specific ability (warhorse, or had their void walker out, for example). I should, as the player, have to find out through engagement or observation what someone is capable of, not through the UI.

My suggestions would be:

- Color-coded name or something that corresponds to both challenge and honor ranking. (Red = impossible kill, tons of honor for a kill. Orange = Unlikely kill, much honor for a kill. Yellow = even-matched, standard honor. Green = easy kill, no honor, or honor loss. Grey = Not worth your time, honor loss). Numeric levels should not even be sent to the client (such that Cosmos et all can't display them).
- Class should not be displayed.
- Race should still be displayed (it's easily observable).

Now, obviously, to balance out warrior bait-and-switch tactics (warrior runs around in a dress, switches to plate when he engages someone), you'd have to build in gear-switching limitations. I believe Blue Text has already stated that there will limitations on armor switching when in combat. Great, solves all those problems.

I think this would really round out PvP, and would put a lot of thrill into it for a lot of people.

 95. Re: Mask classes in PvP,and group levels by c | 10/20/2004 8:34:27 PM GMTDT
I just want to further expound on my earlier point.

Any kind of information hiding steepens the learning curve for PvP, which makes it exponentially harder for casual gamers. In order to perform better in PvP, you need to find out WHY you lost. The more information you lack as to why you lost and the more murky what happens during a fight is, the harder it is to do better the next time. Hardcore players overcome this by the shear amount of time spent in the game, they'll tackle that learning curve quickly, and once they're ontop, compound that advantage so they stay on top. But the more confusing things are, the more casual players won't be able to grasp whats going on.

What steep learning curves caused in DAoC was a fundamental breakdown between the people that knew what was going on, understood the flow of a fight and what makes you win and lose fights, and everyone else. And there was an extreme stratification of levels of play, with 5% of the PvP population dominating the other 95%. As someone in the 5%, sure it was fun for me, but it wasn't really fair. The vast majority of players died so fast to a barrage of spells and melee that they never really were able to analyse what caused them to lose and build upon it to not repeat their mistakes.
