Hm. I think you really need to play eq2 for a while to start appreciating the degree to which it sucks.

Your description sort of lacks impact. It's superficial.

I have had a chance to see the game firsthand, and I'd like to impart some of my feelings about it. I will eventually be making a whole review but this'll do for now.

First Impressions

My first impression of eq2 was that it was a game with nice but drab environmentals (think ffxi) and absurdly bad character graphics. Like, some of the worst I have seen since - well, even eq1 characters look better than this.

I know I'm a geek but - this has to be said, the armor in the game makes me want to stab someone in the eyes. It's so bad, it looks like it was made by someone who has never seen armor, period. It's so form fitting that you can make out buttcrack and cleavage, like badly rendered spandex with padding. The amazing thing is they did this with made-for-tv-movie CGI polygon counts. Not only will it be ugly as dog-vomit, but it'll be ugly at 8 frames per second unless you have a high end video card AND a p43200+.

Topping this, the animations are mostly badly joined and stiff, with poor transitions. Seams at shoulders, stiff movements, very awkward running (and sliding). It's real amateur looking stuff.

Characters and Advancement
Unlike EQ, where you start out in the newbie yard with a dagger, a tattered shirt and newbie food - in eq2 you are dropped into a little console-like linear adventure. Do XYZ and you can escape the tutorial.

I eventually figured out how to bypass most of this.

There are um, 15 character classes? So they claim, but everything I hear it sounds like there's really 5. The top end stuff all sort of blends together. Warrior, Defender, Paladin, Shadowknight. Unlike EQ, there's not much difference between a paladin and shadowknight. You don't even get to -become- a shadowknight until level 20, so for the first 20 levels you are the same as a paladin (crusader).

Continuity and Storyline

The growing impression I get, and I think anyone would get playing this game is that it really has nothing to do with everquest. If I were to hazard a guess, I'd say that all the designers of everquest are busy working on projects that don't suck, and would sooner stick their fingers in dogpoop than touch this project.

The storyline, content, apparent backstory and everything else seems like it was written and designed by someone who played everquest for maybe 2 weeks, and then got a list of the 'major npcs' so they could be incorporated (why?)

Content (or lack thereof}

Imagine you are making a sequel to the world's biggest mmorpg. That's a pretty tall order. I would guess that you would at least want to release it with the same amount of content the original has at the inception, some 5 years ago. Not so here.

This game has -2- starting cities. A 'good' city and an 'evil' city. Qeynos and Freeport. All the other cities conveniently got 'blown up.' Oh, and so did the moon, so we don't have to add content for that. Oh, and the gods all fled, or something. So we don't have any of that either.

I'm not making this stuff up.

So anyhow, the world was 'blown up' or something and now it's somehow gone from 3 continents into a bunch of islands. Nevermind how stupid that is, let's stick to gameplay for now.

The overwhelming feeling I got while running around in tiny adventure zones was that the game felt like it was written to work on a PS/2. The zones are tiny. You could literally fit the entire city of freeport, the commonlands, and the associated adventure zones inside of the barrens and have room to wedge a few taurens around it.

Exp loss on death is back, with a vengeance. No break for you mister newbie. It's got the forward-debt system of COH and other games but without any grace period (i.e. free death below level x). Additionally, if someone in your party dies you get exp debt from them. I am sure I don't have to describe any hypothetical situations here - you can imagine how much this sucks.

The game has private housing, and horses. The horses seem interesting, I am sure I'll never have one, but I am told you can fight on horseback.

The private housing is like FFXI if you couldn't actually do anything in the house other than put furniture down and move it around. No mog for you buddy.

Exp is slow and crappy, groups are 85% completely mandatory for just about everything (many mobs are gratuitously linked together and even highlight as a group when selected, to prevent people from avoiding the asshats that are the rest of the player population).

Puzzlingly, and sadly they lifted a page from the FFXI design bible and added those stupid chains where you have to do 'something' that qualifies as a chaining move to complete 'heroic' strikes. I don't know about you, but this sort of thing fills me with bile and revulsion matched only by dance dance revolution and goatse. So for those of you who don't know what ffxi was like, or don't know what ddr or goatse are/were I'll spell it out for you.

To get good dps in a group, you must hit a matching move to one of the choices that pops up when someone does a move that permits a 'chain.' I.e. if a mage casts "lightning Duh", a little symbol of a hammer and a mouth will appear meaning that you can do any hammer moves or any.. mouth.. moves I suppose, to move onto the next 'step' of the heroic combo stupidity.

I just can't do it justice, it's that dumb.

Tradeskills

The first time I made a potion, and I gathered my junk and bought a candle and some water to make a temper with to refine tin I was like "wow that's pretty cool"

As you make an item, you start out at 'crude' and slowly fight the progress meter to make a 'pristine' version. Pristine subcomponents make for easier combines. Crappy subcomponents will cap how good a final product you can make.

Then when I wanted to make a backpack and realized I needed to craft about 12 subcomponents (2 leather (takes 2 'wash' potions), one leather sheet (takes a potion), one leather cord (potion), one tin buckle (potion and refined tin), refined tin (takes a potion)) I started to realize just how much this system sucked. Complex is one thing, but mindcrushingly tedious is just tedious.

To make a crappy tin sword, I would have had to make about 20 subcomponents (I don't think I'm exaggerating)

tin edge
- tin bar
-- tin ore
-- temper potion
- temper potion
tin pommel
- tin bar
-- tin ore
-- temper potion
- temper potion
tin handguard
- tin bar
-- tin ore
-- temper potion
- temper potion
tin grip
- tin bar
-- tin ore
-- temper potion
- rawhide grip
-- stretched rawhide
--- ruined pelt
--- tanning potion
-- wash potion
-- thread (purchased)
- temper potion

I think that's about right. Each of the potions is a gathered item, plus two storebought items that cost about 12c total. Basically making one of these swords will cost you around a silver, which is about 3 times what it would cost to buy from a vendor that rips you off.

Obviously some of this is a scaling problem, but some of it is linear. The consumables were 6c but higher level consumables might cost more. That's unknown.

I will say I appreciated the complexity at first. I think they might have had the right idea but the implementation is not great. It's due for an overhaul soon, they say - so, I guess we'll see.

The worst part

I have to say, the worst part of the eq beta are the people they found to test it. Maybe this was a skewed sample, but I was filled with murderous hatred from the moment I heard the first snide comment on OOC. Head-bobbing fanboy asshats whose universal answer to every criticism and question from anyone was "then go play something else", even when that wasn't appropriate.

It didn't help that OOC was punctuated about once every half hour with some ignorant newbie comment about WoW and putting wow down, while trying to prop up this disaster of a game with invalid comparisons.

So, there you have it. I've tried my best to be fairly complete and give a good impression of what I thought of the game.

I'd be interested to hear conflicting opinions.
