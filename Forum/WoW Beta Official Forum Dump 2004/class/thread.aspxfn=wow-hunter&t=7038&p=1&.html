 1. Pets can add Gameplay Depth | 10/7/2004 4:31:59 AM GMTDT
The following is a series of suggestions for the hunter class, mostly regarding pets.

I'm sure many of you have played Pokemon before, but for those who haven't, it is a game that revolves around adventuring through a world taming and training creatures to fight on your behalf. In that game, many pets had strengths/weaknesses as well as special abilities. Also, the rarity of certain pets made them highly sought after.

With hunters, I think we have a very similar situation. There are many things that we can learn from the pokemon series that I will try to summarize below. Keep in mind, these are points that I think will add to the gameplay of the hunter class.

1) Individuality. At the moment, almost all pets are the same to me. Sure they differ slighty in stats/hps/dmg. But this is not enough. Give pets strengths/weaknesses against certain types of enemies/spells. Let them develop quirks (such as taming a pet that is unusually aggressive).

2) Variety. This is another thing that pokemon does very well. I haven't played their latest versions, but I understand that the number of pokemon ranges in the hundreds. While beasts in wow are also quite numerous, they are typically derived from the same common models (bear/cat/wolf/croc/raptor). A little more variety in this area would be highly appreciated (though I know it's probably too late to add new beast models). If nothing else, create unique variants of each of these creatures. If I am in a particular zone, there is probably only one cat-type creature of my level. Why not make several, each with distinct strengths and weaknesses? Also along these lines, there should be ranged support pets. At the moment, all are melee, and the formula of a tanking pet is simply too restrictive. Perhaps hunters can build themsevles to be tanks, and get ranged support from a pet.

3) Rarity. Simply put, some creatures need to be rare. Perhaps have a quest in which we go on to slay a mighty dragon. Whereas otherclasses will reap material benfits such as loot and weapons, the hunters' true prize is the dragon egg that is now without a parent. As a dwarf hunter, it is really cool to see NE hunters bring in nightsaber panthers, and I hope this concept is expanded upon before release.

4) Pet customization and development. As pets advance, give us the ability to allocate their stats, and not just have them incremented naturally. This will give us the ability to distinguish two pets tamed from identical creature templates. It will also give the hunter more incentive to keep old pets instead of ditching at the first sight of a more "popular" pet. For example, if I want to create a high damage pet, I may want to tame it at a very low level so I have more control over how it allocates its stats and where. Arrange the pet's skill in a tree so that the hunter must make decisions regarding customizing pets to tailor them to specific play styles.

5) Dueling. Give hunters the ability to pit their pets in duels. That is, the owners cannot intervene. Fights won in this way should give experience to the pet. This would not only be a fun way to compare pets, but would also be a nice show for people to watch in any city.

The pet really is the center of the hunter class. It is what distinguishes it. I think it is far more important to flesh out the pet concept than to concentrate on hunter talents first. I would support making us less powerful and making the pet more powerful. Many of the posts I've read harp on balancing the pet to make it useful in combat. All the points I've made here are more "qualitative" issues that I think can dramastically increase the depth of the hunter class.

I'll try to address the main issues that have been raised so far in a single post.

First, the issue about computing power. There is no doubt that blizzard is using a strong inheritence model for creatures. Let's take the example of crocolisks in the wetlands. I don't know how blizzard is actually organizing their inheritance hierarchy, but it might look something like this:

Wetlands Crocolisk inherits from Crocolisk (superclass)
Young Wetlands Crocolisk inherits from Crocolisk (superclass)
Crocolisk may inherit form an even more generic superclass (beast).

If there is a named Wetlands Crocolisk, then it would inherit from Wetlands Crocolisk. When a class inherits another class, it is given the chance to override some of the parents' properties such as stats, abilities, etc.

Each creature in a zone is an instance of a creature class. Your idea of all creatures of a single type referring to the same creature in memory cannot be true (debuffing an instance of a monster does not debuff every instance of that monster). So, having a zone full of 2 types of creatures versus having a zone full of 50 types of creatures will take up about the same amount of resources in memory. The only added cost is perhaps the database entry or class defintion that provides the properties of the superclass. Long story short, from a programming model, creature variety does not incur a large performance hit (rendering them on your client is another issue, but to the server, they are just objects in memory).

Regarding the argument that hunters are more important than their pets. I feel that pets the most distinguishing point about the hunter. When you ask a wow player to summarize a hunter, the pet is probably the first thing that comes to mind. I hope that by discussing the points here, I've shown that putting more work into the pet is a worthwhile effort to make a hunter a more distinct and unique class. By simply giving him talents and boosting his stats will homogenize him with the rest of the melee classes. Ranged combat may be an exception here.
