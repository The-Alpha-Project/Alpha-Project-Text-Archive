 15. Re: intelligence vs spirit | 8/3/2004 11:44:18 PM GMTDT
"1 int = 9 mana

1 spirit = .25 mana per tick.

So it takes 36 ticks before 1 spirit surpasses 1 int in usefulness, during the course of a battle. I don't remember exactly how long a tick is, but it does mean that a battle would have to last over a minute for spirit to be better then int."

Honestly the 2 cannot be described as a function of each other in any way. Int is strictly maximum mana, until it is correlated with crit percent next patch. At this point in time, a Druid with high enough Spirit (relative to level) never has to drink while exping. If you have an add you can't handle, you're running regardless. Anything short of that falls into the constraints of efficiency, regardless of whether you have 1k mana or 5k mana, if your Spirit is half enough to nuke on the pull, flip Moon and Rejuv back and forth until the mob is dead and move onto the next one with near full health/mana, Int is entirely worthless. That is the extreme end of the argument, that efficiency is defined by Spirit and Strength alone; everything else is a modifier to maximum and technically does nothing for you, but the true benefit of Int is in instances where it is quite appreciated. Therein lies the source of what you're seeing about the lack of Druid friendly Leather in the higher end; high Int gear is very welcome for instances when you're going to be drinking (often summoned drinks at no cost to you) very often, and your described relationship between the benefits of Int and Spirit do actually come into play, and thats where any hardcore Spirit Druid will concede the point, it's not very valuable in instances that should give you a challenge relative to your level. Thats generally a set of gear in a bag tho, it does nothing for you while soloing or in an exp group.

Additionally, both of our most efficient heals have a 12 second timer to do their work after they are casted, thus making time out for drinking inconsequential. This is a very important factor to take into account in this argument, it tips the scales heavily. It takes very stressful (unconventional) circumstances to really make the Int argument make sense outside of instances.
