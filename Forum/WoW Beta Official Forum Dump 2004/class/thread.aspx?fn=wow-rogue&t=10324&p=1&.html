 4. Re: What is are the cons of an off hand weapon? | 9/29/2004 8:22:14 PM GMTDT
Normal miss rate with no Dual Wield skill is 5%. Miss rate with Dual Wield is 24%.

So you miss 19% more often. However your off hand weapon will do 50% damage per hit.

So a net gain of 31% damage. Then you can spec talents to get dual wield specialization which brings you damage up to 56% more damage.

The negative side effect however is that when attacking MoBs 3+ levels over you, your miss rate goes though the roof and that extra 19% miss rate can effectively drop you DPS to almost Nil.

You can spec percision to give you back 5% to hit but you will still be at 3 times the miss rate of a non-dual wield rogue.

On MoBs your level or lower, Dual Wield tears though them like they are made of rice paper though.

Anyway, those are the pros and cons.

Q u o t e:
In ad&d I was able to backstab with a 2h sword. It has a pointy end..why not?


Because "backstabbing" isn't simply stabbing someone in the back. It's meant to be an assassination-type thing where a rogue will strike at a specific point to inflict as much damage possible. Highly difficult with a clumsy two-hander or anything but a dagger.

Q u o t e:
I think there should be different classifications of weapons besides "swords, axes, daggers, etc". Instead it should be 'piercing', 'slashing', blunt, etc.


While I agree, it's pretty much the same as it is now. If you mean to have rogues classify as a "piercing, slashing, and blunt" class, rogues simply don't use axes because they're not appropriate. Axes are made for hewing stuff like armour or wood. Rogues rely on control and accuracy (probably why agility is their main stat). Although daggers are archetypically rogue, short swords make for excellent stealthy close combat as do bludgeons like maces (think sap, and mace stuns as baby saps).

Q u o t e:
Some swords like a rapier is a pointy object, and more like a dagger then a sword. I think those types of swords should be backstabbable.


Rapiers are not daggers. While they are thrusting weapons, they are used at an extended range and were actually designed to keep opponents as far away as possible due to their long blade (as well as disable the opponents themselves through quick strikes to the hand or whatnot). Tip control is vital here. With a shorter blade, the user has much more control over the tip and is thus far more accurate. It's why the ricasso is used on rapiers, although they don't have nearly the accuracy of a dagger.
