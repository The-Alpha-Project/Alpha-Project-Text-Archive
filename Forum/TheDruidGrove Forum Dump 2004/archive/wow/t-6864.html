There are a few classes in EQ that historically have tended to see a lot more of the game as a whole than others, one of which is the Druid. I know for a fact that had I picked almost any other class back in 1999, I wouldn't have seen nearly as much of Everquest as I have with Riggen. With him I've been to almost every nook and cranny of every zone outside of Time, seen every sight, and fought every encounter. Some places solo, some grouped, some in raids. Many over time in all three. I've gotten a lot of mileage out of the game thanks to my class.

The shapeshifting aspect of Druids has attracted me since the days of AD&D, and I think it can be a distinctive vehicle for bringing out that can-do go-anywhere ability in WoW Druids. I'm glad to see Blizzard emphasizing this aspect of their gameplay. I'm hopeful that they've examined the class dynamics of EQ and have taken note of what to avoid and what to embrace.

The trick I think to keeping from falling into the "underpowered" trap while avoiding stepping on the toes of more focused classes is to deliberately create some unique synergies among the basic abilities of Druids. Based on comments from the interview that Sobe conducted with Blizzard it sounds a lot like this is the route that they're going to travel.

Here's hopin'.

Fewer classes means more possible roles for each class, so versatility need not come with such a steep cost as it has in EQ. Look at how Blizzard appears to be wrapping the Enchanter and Cleric--two of the most historically desirable classes--into one. That's a lot of power to balance against.

Bear in mind also the skill tree based advancement system in WoW. Just because Druids have a lot of different abilities doesn't necessarily mean that they'll be able to become powerful in all of them at once. Especially at higher levels a skill tree could make versatility versus focused ability a self-correcting problem. We may simply see lots of different kinds of Druids.
