Background for those that don't know me.
I played EQ since late 1999. I have a built in group of friends I play WoW with. When they aren't on, I'll solo. Hardcore. Raiding is last on my list of to do's. I love to quest. I love to test the limits of the class.

I wish this opinion was from the L60 viewpoint, but I've only played in the final stress. I've tinkered with questing, trades and a few other classes. Lots of fun. I have a Druid up to L19 at the moment and here is my take. If I've missed something, or have my facts wrong please jump in and let me know.

If you are soloing shape shifting is a highly viable option. I say this because others have said and I agree that shifting shapes doesn't work well in groups. When soloing for XP I find myself moving in and out of bear form regularly and without any problem. It's fun and the halmark of a WoW Druid. Chain killing mobs is efficient and fun. The WoW Druid with Bear form is an efficient killing machine, solo. Then again, I hear all classes can solo efficiently.

As I said from my personal experience, shape shifting in groups doesn't work well. My DPS drags in Bear form because the mob is heavily damaged by the time it even arrives on pull then when it runs I have to chase it. Why bother? Just stay in regular form and nuke. You can't cast any spells in bear form and if you change to Druid form to cast a spell then go back to Bear form all the rage you had banked up is gone. For anyone that hasn't played beta, Rage is like mana for melee special moves only it starts out at zero and goes up as you attack mobs. If you aren't in combat it quickly dissipates -- annoying.

I don't have cat form yet - L20. I wonder how that DPS compares. I've only tinkered with combo points with a pure class Rogue, so we'll see. Looks like fun but I wonder how diluted it is.

Talents. Pft, Talents! If I'm going to be a grouping Druid why wouldn't I pick all of the healing talent options? Healing in groups seems to be our first calling like it or not. Am I wrong? So -- it seems like suicide to pick all the cool Feral and Nuking balance options then to try and go into a group as a healer. So, how do I really feel about talents? If you do nothing but solo, you'll love it. If you like to group you're going to be upset that you're picking all the healing talent options when you're rather be picking the others...unless you really like healing, then whey didn't you just make a Priest?

Conclusions
If you're a Druid (you Druids know what I mean), you're probability going to play a Druid in WoW. That said, I know many of you other long-time Druids aren't willing to go through what we did in EQ. You love the class flexibility even if you loath not being as powerful as the class you mimic. Those of us that stay with a Druid are going to hope Blizzard fixes the class enough that the perception or reality of the class not being first choice group worthy goes away. We'll see.

Regards,

I can add my own thoughts to this thread, since I've been in beta since June (I think) and leveled up to 60 on the closed beta server.

To add to your rage comments--rage is built up based on each time you get hit; the more you get hit, the faster it builds. Cat form abilities takes energy; when you shift, your bar isn't full, but it builds up pretty quickly. As you use an ability, it drops and then gradually builds again.

DPS is great at lower levels with bear form, and goes up when you get cat form. If I dumped all my mana into a fight with nukes in caster form, then I could get up over bear form damage, but it never struck me as a very efficient means to play. You can deal great damage in forms while regenning mana, and pop out to help heal or whatever when/if needed. Meleeing in caster form with a DoT and regen on yourself is possible, but a bit slower than when shifted.

Tanking--
Yup, I've been the main tank several times, especially at lower levels (up to about level 40 or so). Around level 40 and higher, other classes get mail, and then plate armor, which will leave you taking hits from mobs harder than they will. In other words, you're no longer the best choice for a tank, and groups often prefer you as a healer for that reason. Perhaps this changes if you are fully feral-spec'd.

My personal opinion--we need an AC/blocking ability boost, and it would be lovely if our weapon damage was used in forms (currently, it's not--your forms are set at a base damage rate, and so while other classes get upgrades in damage from their weapons as they acquire new items, we don't when in forms).

Cat form is insanely fun, although I noticed that my damage started to drop off in comparison to caster damage as I leveled, so I rarely used cat form (unless I was scouting with Prowl). Regardless, you'll find that you can tear through mobs when you first get cat form and having a blast.

I really hate to say this, but I'm having serious doubts that I will play a druid at retail. I don't mind healing (and only healing/buffing) on raids and instances, but I'm doing a better job of that with my priestess. Since that's all I'm wanted for (mostly) at level 60, I've set aside my druid now as an alt for when I want to play around on my own or with a friend.

Just to reiterate--this is totally my opinion, and I'm sure there are druids out there that might disagree with me. The class is a ton of fun to play, so I'll definately have a druid alt...but since I only heal in druid at level 60, I've decided that the priestess fits better with my playstyle.

Keep in mind that I never tried a druid in WoW, but I've partied with them, and read a lot about em. Played one in EQ since that game was released, etc etc...

Seems to me that the druid class in WoW is fated to be as useless in the end game as the EQ druid is. The bottom line is... if you make a class designed to fill 3 roles... then you have 3 other classes each with those roles but specialized in them... then you cant ever make the fleixble single class as good as the specialized class in that role. Otherwise what would be the point of the other 3 classes?

It seems to me that a multi-role class only shines when there are more roles needed than there are people available/allowed to fill those roles. The druid worked reasonably well in EQ for that in many situations, to varying degrees throughout the life of EQ. One reason for this is that the specialist classes really were very narrowly defined in EQ (until recently) so that the extra flexibility could be an asset - at least in single group situations. In raids it simply doesnt have any value, because the specialist is always available to do the required job better.

So in WoW, you've got the druid class, supposedly with these 3 roles. They seem to work quite well for the first 20 levels... the next 20 are kinda iffy... and by the end, you are basically reduced to what an EQ druid is reduced to - a second rate healer with anemic dps and a buff that people like but can easily do without. And unlike EQ, there's no grinding for AA's at the end of your level path (for good or bad), so the impetus for doing single group encounters is lessened in everyone who reaches max level. The end game of WoW seems like it's going to revolve around raiding, and that means specialists are needed. The WoW druid may fill a niche role in the WoW raiding game, yes, but will that be enough? Or will there be more endless heartache about the loss of relative power that relying on class flexibility makes inevitable?

The journey will no doubt be enjoyable... WoW is a heck of a lot of fun. But what worries me about the druid class in WoW is the feeling that the time will be wasted in the end, that if I still want to play and be useful at level 60, however long it takes me to get there, that I would have been better off playing a different class, or that I'll again wind up fighting a never-ending battle for endgame relevance.

I'll probably never play another game as much as I played EQ, so it's mostly academic to me really... but I do have to wonder whether it'll turn out the same way for the druid class.

On the other hand, my huntress has even more issues than that, and I found her to be the most fun of the classes I did try hehe... so who knows, eh?

I've been trying to make a comparison between warriors and druids with respect to tanking. I've heard over and over that at high levels druids fall short of being a decent tank, but based on the skills and items available it seems to me that they should be at least close in abilities.

The first aspect of tanking that has gotten a lot of discussion is the armor (leather vs plate). Now at first glance the leather armor seems WAY lower than plate, but if you apply the AC bonus of say Dire Bear (+125% according to thottbot) the difference becomes much more reasonable. For example, I compared the normal plate armor to the reinforced leather armor, both level 45, both vendor purchasable. Adding up the numbers for all the armor slots (no shield) yields 2662 for plate and 758 for leather (without any other bonuses). Now if you modify the leather armor to reflect dire bear form you get 1705 armor, about 1k short of plate, but not really horrible. Interestingly, if you add an armor kit to the four allowed pieces you get +160 armor (i think the best armor kit if 40 per piece). I'm not positive if the bear form AC modify applies to these armor kits, since they show up in green on your char sheet, but if they do then that makes the final number 2822 for plate and 2065 for leather/bear. That's less than 800 difference... which seems pretty good for leather.

Now aside from the leather issue, there are the skills available to warriors and druids. It seems to me, just from looking at skills lists, that I would actually prefer the bear skills over a warrior's... simply because a bear doesn't need to worry about stances. From what I can tell a warror can't charge during combat unless they are in berserk stance and can't taunt unless in defensive stance, both of which a druid can do in bear form. The warrior certainly gets more skills to use during melee, but I can't see myself using more than a handful during a fight... rage doesn't generate THAT fast and I would probably save it for stuns/interrupts and occasional mauls. On top of that, the druid gets to pull with spells like wrath or, more idealy, moonfire. At high levels moonfire is a much better DOT than the rend warrior ability and can be cast at range. There's also MOTW, thorns, and the heals that can be used in a pinch...

So after looking at all of the abilities/shortcomings of both warriors and feral druids I can't say as though a druid looks as bad as every seems to claim. For a reasonable deduction in armor a druid can buff himself and a group, heal in a pinch, travel faster (cheetah), etc etc... so why wouldn't a group want a feral druid as their tank?

Are there any high level druids who have specialized in feral bear abilities and tank equipment who can tell me the problems with a high level bear tank? Do these numbers actually mean anything or is there something that the stats don't show?

I played a priest in closed beta. There is a ton of difference between a plate wearing MT and a druid in bear form MTing. If you take a druid as a tank, you have to be really careful on the pulls, take it slow. Quite a lot of mana is going to be used keeping the druid alive. Levels 45-55 I'd say they tank almost on the level of a rogue with taunt moreso than the warrior or paladin.

Druids are great off tanks, good backup healers. A lot of the issues with the class boil down to 1) gear/stats for the different forms (including weapons factoring for dps) 2) generalists need to be more like 80/80/80 (or more) of the parent class. There needs to be enough of a difference so that druids don't replace, say, the warrior, but not so much that performing the role is all but impractical.

As it stands right now, druids are not close to that 80/80/80 ballpark for the shapeshifting forms. Caster form is pretty decent, cat form comes nowhere close to rogue in dps or abilities. Bear form doesn't have the AC (nor the block/parry/dodge) needed to make them a good fallback option if no warriors can be found.

However, for the soloing/duoing/questing for the more casual playstyle, druids are quite able to handle themselves and their groupmates. Pretty much every druid that I talked to that loved the class had the more casual style, thus weren't really confronted with the problems you would encounter in the upper tier of instances. Right now I'd say that if you are NOT aiming for serious end game instance runs and raiding then the druid is a very good choice to play. This could easily change though with just a few changes to the class.

Overall, I'd give the class a B-.
