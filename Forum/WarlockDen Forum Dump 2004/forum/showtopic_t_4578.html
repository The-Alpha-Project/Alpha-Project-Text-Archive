Would it surprise you to know I’ve been playing a lot of World of Warcraft? No, I thought not. Given twenty minutes of free time, you can pretty much bank on my having logged into Azeroth to complete some leg of a quest, work up a few points in a tradeskill, explore a new town, or even just kill some wandering baddies for the sheer joy of it. What I’ll be curious to see in the long run is how much my enthusiasm for WoW holds up; whether my current mania is a function of a well crafted game – even in its current beta state – or fueled by my long standing anticipation to play?

With that somewhat interrogatively ambiguous introduction at hand, let’s move into the second World of Warcraft diary. Or, if you missed our first WoW Diary, you can find it here.

Enthusiasm for a new game is nothing new to me. Whenever a game that I’ve been anticipating for longer than a day and a half comes into my meaty grasp I tend to obsess over it for a length of time generally proportional to the game’s overall quality before my more objective side resurfaces and a raging debate of internal monologue leaves me in a virtually catatonic state. During the overenthusiastic period I do take measures to try and not overstate my perspective of the game, despite the near stifling desire to do so, though, given my existing predilection toward hyperbole, it’s hard to say how successful I am at curbing said enthusiasm. So, anything I say about World of Warcraft only two weeks fresh into the beta absolutely needs to be taken with a grain of salt, particularly when I get so adjective happy that my words actually start to glow.

Still, the longer I’m enthusiastic about a game, the better the game tends to look even from an objective point of view. And it’s not like I go fanboy for every piece of software that slides across my desk, so any enthusiasm at all – regardless of its exaggerated nature – has to be taken as a good sign. What all this is leading to is the simple statement that I remain as enamored with the beta as I did last week. Not a particularly notable statement of endorsement, I admit, but considering that I get tired of most MMOGs after about this length of play it remains an encouraging sign.

But in the sake of objectivity – and as a kind of gush aversion therapy - let’s be honest and talk about why the game’s not on store shelves yet.

What’s Taking So Long – I don’t want this to feel like the section where I try to think of things that are wrong with World of Warcraft to convince readers either that I’m a jaded self-important gamer who’s totally above the hype, or that I just like to look for things to complain about. In fact, as you look over this and see that Blizzard does in fact have work to do, you should applaud them for getting it done properly in a beta where this kind of work belongs instead of tossing it out the door and fixing the issues in a convoy of patches. Because, and let’s be perfectly clear about this, World of Warcraft has virtually reached the point of being competitive with any game on the market, and could easily be rushed to stores by the end of June.

That said, smart money has a November release.

There’s a lot to be done in that time before release. There is still one class, the Hunter, which has yet to be put into beta test at all, much less balanced. The current level cap remains at 39 with 60 expected to be the final release cap - a good indication that lots of solid high level content still needs to be developed and put into place. Depending on who you ask, as much as 50% of the world still remains unavailable, again largely comprised of higher level zones. Larger scale PvP, aside from individually requested duels, and the servers designed for Faction vs. Faction play have yet to be implemented. Mounts, racial bonuses and auction houses are yet to see the light of day. Distribution of talent points (an alternative character development system) is forthcoming and, I must say, highly anticipated. The list goes on and on.

But, there’s more than just the nuts and bolts to be worked in and worked out. While many of the previews you’ve probably read are all flowers and hugs, the beta community is, as every beta community tends to be, a mix of enthusiasm, suggestions, complaints, flames, whines, cheers, and occasionally literate thoughts on the state of the game thus far. People are talking about a variety of issues, among them some lag problems; an abundance of quests that seem too linear and eventually a little stale; a lack of diversity among characters, classes, and races; simplistic item, weapon, and skill system implementation; profitable zones rife with exploits; gaining levels too quickly (if you can believe it); and an over inflated, limping player economy. To be honest, there are legitimate concerns to be made about each of the issues above – though not every expressed concern proves legitimate, and at some point we have to talk about the problem children of WoW's community – and Blizzard, in the flurry of activity to get the content they’ve promised ready for testing, is at the same time busy addressing the wealth of issues and imbalances that crop up from their already produced content.

Blizzard is still very much in the big patch mode – known here as a ‘push’ – of beta testing where major updates come in the time frame of months, and then the deluge of feedback is dealt with. This is not the open beta environment where each day sees a little tweak here, some adjustment there. WoW is still being built and rebuilt in massive chunks, though that time seems to be drawing to a close. Simply put, Blizzard is at a crossroads that every MMOG faces. Were they to jump the gun, the client is truly stable enough to talk about impending release, but to be true to their vision and fans Blizzard simply must sit on WoW for another half year to get it up to the standards the market expects.

I sincerely hope that by mentioning what needs work it don’t come off as a laundry list of complaints. It’s not meant to taint my, or for that matter your perspective of the game, but to serve as answer for those who’ve been asking why the game isn’t nearing gold. Instead of worrying about what might come from any of the issues, applaud Blizzard’s dedication to putting a finished product on the shelves, and try to be patient.

There is one other hot topic I haven't mentioned yet at the front of the beta test, but I thought it was better served as its own discussion.

Take a Break! – The inclusion of Rest States in World of Warcraft is a point of extreme controversy. It works fundamentally like this: the longer you play your character the less experience you receive from killing things until you log out at an Inn for eight hours to ‘rest’ – though an upcoming push promises a more flexible rest recovery system. There are five states of rest: well rested gets 200% experience, rested 150%, normal 100%, fatigued 50%, and exhausted only gets 25% experience per kill. Some people complain that Blizzard is trying to engineer the lifestyle of their customers, that it penalizes those players most dedicated to World of Warcraft, that it is put in place just to please the casual gamer, and/or that it artificially extends the level cap. It shouldn’t be any surprise that those most vocal about the rest state idea tends to be those most likely to spend six hours a day playing the game.

Some people have probably told you it’s a big deal, even a make or break issue for the game. It’s not. Truth be told, a casual gamer will probably barely notice the rest state’s existence. Having played a character up to level 15 in what I’d consider a relatively flurry of activity, I’ve only found myself not in a Well Rested state two or three times, rarely for long, and never have I even come close to reaching ‘Normal’. As long as you logout periodically in an inn for the night, you’ll be hard pressed to notice it’s there, much less have your experience accumulation seriously modified. In the end, it may well be a leveling bar by which to keep powergamers in some kind of moderate check, but they can cry me a river. It’s a smart move, and an innovative idea. It encourages those who can’t invest regular extended periods of time in the game without really penalizing anyone. I certainly can’t see how anyone could play down to a fatigued or exhausted level unless they simply refused to log out at an inn, or they were having person after person playing a single character without logging out.

It would be pretty hard for me to devise any serious complain against the idea. Unless you plan to play your character eight or ten hours at a time and rarely log off for any consecutive stretch, then it’s really a non-issue.

So Much To See – On a completely unrelated topic, I want to talk briefly about the actual world I’ve been playing in. You’ve seen the screenshots and words have gushed on the art style of WoW. Leave it sufficed to say for now that the gush is entirely deserved. But, one of the things that most often bugs me about new MMOGs is the decided lack of depth in the world. Rarely, particularly outside the most crowded areas, does the landscape of these decidedly massive worlds feel particularly lived in.

I recall when Star Wars Galaxies launched, how much a waste of space the planets felt outside of the cities. There would be a giant city, and speckled around the city would be a handful of notable pieces of landscape worth visiting, and between them vast land expanses of varying elevation. There’s nothing quite as underwhelming, and dare I say discouraging, as cresting the rise of a tall hill to see a series of slightly different sized but otherwise identical hills beyond. And let’s not even speak of Asheron’s Call 2, the premise of which being that the entire planet had been left in barren desolation – a handy and ultimately prophetic excuse that was.

And so, the thing that has most pleased me so far in beta experience has been the diversity of unique landmarks that speckle the zones I’ve visited. Beyond the great cities of Stormwind, and Ironforge, or even the satellite towns there are farms, mines, houses, towers, encampments, docks, quarries, lumber mills, windmills, barns, graveyards, broken debris from long fought battles, sunken ships, castles, waterfalls, pumpkin patches, and a thousand other set pieces that lend substance to the world. There is an entire team of people dedicated to taking the landscapes and breathing life into them. They give a sense of place and purpose, and I tip my hat to them.

As I wrap up this second diary, I hope in some way that I’ve communicated a somewhat more realistic view of how far Blizzard has come in developing their game, but at the same time how much more they have planned. There are, as there should be in beta, a number of issues in the game that must be tweaked or fixed, and with each adjustment twice as many new issues are likely to develop. For those who wonder why Blizzard takes so long in releasing game, it seems to be because they have the sense and resources to dedicate an appropriate length of time to polishing their work. Far too often, once the technical hurdles have been cleared, games end up on a fast track to release, and the worlds we are given can’t sustain either our imagination or our suspension of disbelief. It’s good to see Blizzard remembering that ‘when it’s ready’ means more than just ‘when it works.’

- Elysium
