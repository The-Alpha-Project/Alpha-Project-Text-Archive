So I was given this prompt:
"Over the next couple of weeks, we'll look at essays which talk about experiences which shape literacy and the writers' understanding about how literacy has shaped them. The purpose of this paper is to examine your own history as a writer, reader and/or communicator and think about a moment or moments which have influenced, for better or worse or both, your literacy and how and what you think about it..."

and this is what came out...

Through a Keyboard

Once upon a time not too long ago, I was an Everquest player. Everquest a MMORPG (Massively Multiplayer Online Role Playing Game) relies on communication solely through a keyboard. Thus making any kind of real emotion hard to express and even harder to read.

I, at this point in my story, had been playing with a group of people for about a year and a half. These people, hailing from all parts of the world and all types of backgrounds and professions, were my friends and fellow cohorts for our adventures online. We had grown very close, both as fellow players and friends. It got to the point where I knew the names of the other players wives, sisters, brothers, and although it was rarely admitted we had them, parents. We all knew who the doctors and lawyers were, who was still in school, what classes they were taking, the sports we all played, and the things that we really cared about in, what was affectionately called “The Real World”.

My usual group of players had been working in a rather long and difficult area for some time and as suck all of us were feeling the effects of many hours of long work, with little pay off. When we all decided that we had been playing long enough for that particular night, having played 4 hours in roughly the same area. There was something that was not right, there was an element of our group that was missing, a factor that was always there and always appreciated, but was hard to really pinpoint. At first I had thought that one of our usual group members had not logged on that night to play, but after checking the roster and seeing that indeed everyone was present and accounted for, I thought that this strange emptiness was simply my mind playing tricks on me in retaliation for the long period of abuse. I bid everyone fare well and called it a night, ignoring the odd feelings that were still slowly creeping into my mind.

The next day we continued on, still that same feeling of emptiness hanging in the air like a dark cloud before a terrible storm. Finally determined to solve this odd paradox and with the knowledge that everyone was present, I pushed myself back from the computer desk and looked at the screen for a moment. The usual people were in the usual places; everyone was doing what they were suppose to be doing. The group was, it seemed, normal. The tanks, those characters that take the brunt of the damage that is done by the monster, were up front getting pounded on. The casters, those wielding magics and using spells to return that damage back to the monsters, were there casting their spells. The priests, using their amazing healing skills, were trying desperately to keep the tanks alive and then, although not as often as everyone would have liked, there would be the dead monster.

Suddenly, as if a ton of bricks had landed on my wee little brain, it came to me. One of the most important parts of our group, one of our members, one of US, was not talking. They were where they were supposed to be, doing what needed to be done, but the humor and life had left her. The all too familiar victory dance atop the defeated monster, the not rhyming rhymes that were giving at the end telling of our grand victory, the smiles and laughs expressed as lol’s and hehe’s, were all gone.

Instead, she was hanging at the back of the group, sitting and pretending to be busy with some other chore of great importance, standing at just the right time to join in the fight, much unlike someone that was busy with some other task.

Now knowing what was going on I told the rest of the group that I was going to be away from my keyboard for a minute and that if anyone had had a beef with me that this was now the time to “train” a monster on to me and kill my virtual self.

I then sat down and put the “AFK” tag above my head, a virtual visual reminder that I was not present. Now the group taken care of, I sent her a private message, one that only she and I would be able to read and respond to, asking if everything was ok. Naturally in the odd way the internet works with human interaction, everything was fine and there be no need for concern. Of course that answer was unacceptable. So I asked again noting the changes in play style and behavior of my long time friend, and after a long pause, she finally broke down.

She began to tell me of the plight that her father was in back in her home country, how he was in the hospital after being in a terrible car accident, and also of how there was nothing that she or any of her family could do to help him, other then send their love and prayers.

We talked for what seemed like hours, her telling me of how much she loved him and cared for his strong hands that had raised her from a girl into a woman. How she wanted so much to do something or to go and see him, and that because of circumstances she could not leave the United States. She told me of the accident, of how and where it happened, describing the street where she grew up on that the two cars collided. I could feel her pain and anguish, her misery and despair all flooding into me, hurting as deep as it was hurting her. This person, this friend, this sister, hurting so much, was in turn hurting me.

I gave her all the comfort one can give to someone in that situation, telling her that it would be ok, that he was a strong man, and a good man. That her father was not a quitter, that he had stayed with her mother even when things got their worst, he did the right thing, and that it would be ok.

I let her tell me of all the things that she felt, of all the good times they had shared together. How all of the memories came flooding back to her but most importantly, she talked to me of her love for him and her dearest hope that she should see him one more time and tell him how she loved him so.

The rest of the group noticing my absences for so long began to ask question of my whereabouts and of hers. So after what seemed the correct amount of time, I addressed the group and told them of a most dire situation involving a pan and my cat that had to be attended to before I would be able to play again, and as such was why I had taken so long. To much jeers and cracks, the group took this excuse without question, and we all continued on.

After a week’s time had gone by, the group successfully accomplishing the task at hand, I was helping some members of our group with a different endeavor and got a private tell from her asking if I had a moment or two to speak with her.

Bidding those I was with goodbye I found a nice safe place to sit and talk and asked if everything was ok. She told me that her father pulled through, and that she hadn’t been on these past few days because she was finally able to go see him in the hospital. Overjoyed personally, I related to her how incredibly happy I was for both her and her father and that the next time that she speaks with him to please make sure he knows that I send my best. We talked further about what had happened and thank you’s and such were given for being there when needed and picking up on the fact that something was indeed wrong. I thanked her for letting me know and that I was glad to hear he was going to make a full recovery.

Later that day, I found myself feeling much better, not only about the situation with her father, but also about everything around me. The world seemed fuller and more alive then it had ever before, a sense, of well being presided over me as I went about my day-to-day things outside of the game.

Even to this day, some years later now, I still keep in close contact with all of the people that I became such close friends with through Everquest. Most of these people I have never seen, nor will ever meet in “Real Life”, but it dose not matter, we are friends, the kind that you always want to have, the kinds of friends that no matter what life throws your way, you can get on-line and have a chat with them about it. Those people that I met through a computer screen and a keyboard while playing a game, are some of my closest friends, and some, while only a select few, are indeed part of my family. They have helped raised me and taught me to be who I am today and I in turn have helped shaped who they are and the truly odd thing is that most of us have never even spoken one word to the other. Everything, the feelings, the emotions, the pain and joy, has all been filtered through that one single keyboard.


so yea... just felt like I should share it for some reason....


stay safe everyone,
