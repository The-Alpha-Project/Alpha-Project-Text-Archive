I think it's time for me to submit my first beta journal. I've only been playing WoW since last Saturday (5 days now), but I've seen and experienced enough in that time to compose a rudimentary journal of my findings. I will be focusing mostly on warlock-specific content, but I'll cover a bit about quests and general gameplay as well. So here goes... (no screenies yet, but I'll post them later)

First off, the cut-scene is a nice piece of polish, I must say. Laying out the foundation of the undead race - it's motivations and beliefs - lends a feeling of purpose to your initial character experience. I've played several other mmorpg's, and none have laid out the character's race and overall attitude in the way that Blizzard chose to do so.

After resetting my movement keys to make them more comfortable, I began my questing. Everyone talks about how you hardly have to move 5 feet before you see the ' ! ' signifier of a quest, and they're all correct. I was so excited to finally be in the beta, that I initially forgot all about finding the imp pet quest-giver and just head out on my own. Of course, early mobs are easy to solo, so it wasn't until I was level 2 1/2 that I even realized it. At that point I found the NPC, completed the quest, and gained my imp. From there, things got even easier. On a humorous note, run by a mob with your pet in tow and let it be attacked, and it comes out with one-liners such as 'Hey! Are you going to help me out here or what?'. A nice touch, and a good laugh the first few times you see it.

Moving on to a broader scope, let me explain some of my opinions on questing and general gameplay. In the early stages of character development, there is absolutely no need whatsoever to seek out a group to accomplish any quests or to grind some xp and gain levels. I have mixed feelings about this the way it stands now. On one hand, I'm an EQ veteran. I used to play a warrior exclusively, and there were lots of times where I logged off because I couldn't find a group. And in EQ, not finding a group means you can't do squat when you're a melee class... unless you want to tradeskill a bit (which is also cryptic and confusing in EQ without going to sites with recipes and spoiler content). Anyhow... I digress. There's no need to seek companionship early in WoW, as I was saying. And though this appeals to my senses of 'I don't wanna have to rely on others to play', it also means that WoW plays like a massively single-player online game in the beginning. It's also important to mention that there's minimal downtime as a warlock, even when soloing all damn day! I've leveled to 17 at this time, and the need for groups to complete quests is beginning to become more apparent, though it's still not mandatory. Of course, I'm sure this is also dependent upon class, and that's what I'll explain now.

Being a warlock means lots of things. You can self-heal with healthstones and life tap. You can nuke (barely), DoT (damage over time), curse, you have pets, and you can melee just fine most of the time (if for no reason other than to get your skill up). I'd like to take this opportunity to completely DISAGREE with lots of other warlock players, and set the record straight about soloing as a warlock... and by 'set the record straight', I mean 'straight' in my humble opinion. Lots of people complain that the voidwalker (first available at level 10 by completing a quest) is insufficient as a tank, and can't hold aggro. I couldn't disagree more.

What I found was simple: take the 'taunt' ability of the VW (voidwalker, not volkswagon) off auto, and do it manually. But you have to be a bit more selective about *when* you use it. For example, if I'm soloing with my VW and cast Shadowbolt, there's a HUGE difference in aggro control based on exactly when you taunt. The general consensus is to taunt right after the shadowbolt hits, which should turn the mob from you back to the vw immediately, hence you don't even get hit. That's the way it's worked in lots of other games, and I think that's what people expect in WoW as well. But in trying this many times, and many times getting my posterior fleshbags presented to me (mobs handed my butt to me), I decided to try something different. As I cast shadowbolt, I watch my spell-timer. Just *before* the bolt hits the mob, I manually taunt with the vw. I almost never get hit anymore. That's no joke... almost *never*, with very few exceptions. One of those exceptions is when I crit with my bolt. Doing 91 damage instead of 45 or 50 will nearly always get you some aggro, which is expected, and I wouldn't have it any other way. A typical fight when soloing with my vw goes as such: pull with corruption (instant cast curse DoT - note this is a curse and only one curse can be active at a time), send in vw and manually taunt, cast immolate (non-curse DoT), wait 1 or 2 seconds, cast shadowbolt & taunt just before it hits, continue with shadowbolt / taunt combo and possibly melee. Taunt if and when necessary. That's it.

I'll tell you now, you might as well get yourself a much shorter quick-slot bar for grouping. Here's what I have on my quick-slot bar for grouping: shoot (for my wand), shadowbolt, immolate, curse of weakness. That's it. I do, of course, keep health funnel, life tap, and drain soul up as well, but only the former do any real damage. There's practically no sense in using most of the DoT's because they all do insufficent damage and take too damn long to run their course. A typical fight is over in 15 seconds, sometimes less. That's not enough time for DoT's to be effective. In fact, you're hurting the party's overall DPS by taking up the casting time for those spells instead of nuking. Curse of weakness is nice, of course. At Rank 2 mine makes mobs deal 7 less damage per hit. This adds up nicely, especially if there's a shaman in the party with Stone Skin Totem (depending on level, another 7 less damage per hit). Basically, I cast CoW (weakness), send in vw (I keep vw around in case we get adds, depending on where we're fighting), and then immolate, shadowbolt, shadowbolt, shadowbolt. In nearly all fights, I don't even get the third shadowbolt off, but I'm hoping things will get tougher as I level up more. The only reason I even use immolate is because it deals initial damage when it lands, plus it adds a DoT. Additionally, if we're fighting in an area that crowd control isn't needed, I summon my imp to give the party Blood Pact (HP increase) instead of keeping the voidwalker around. I also hand out minor healthstones to every party member right away. I usually drain soul from the first few mobs we take out, then ask them to hold while I cast, and hand them out. I seem to have made a good name for myself by doing this, and have been added to many friends lists and invited to plenty of groups lately.

Now, there's one severe criticism I simply can't pass up about the warlock. He needs MORE NUKES! Shadowbolt is basically your bread and butter. It's a nice spell and all, but it just gets a bit boring after awhile to be so limited. I mean... seriously... a demon-summoning, black-magic wielding, goody-two-shoes hating warlock oughtta have some *I hate you now and I hate you HARD* spells in his or her repertoire.

Speaking of limited.... I'm not going to bother tradeskilling in the beta. There's a ton of people doing it, and I'd rather spend my time play-testing (which is, after all, the purpose of the beta) than tradeskilling. So... I made a somewhat ridiculous decision: I have spent 100 skill points to train in the use of swords!!! I did this for three reasons:

1. I'm certain Blizzard won't let us keep beta characters, so I might as well try it out on a character that's a 'throw-away' instead of a real character that I wanna build up. If I end up hating it, it would suck to have sunk that many skill points into it.
2. I've not seen a single solitary warlock who uses them, and I just gotta be different.
3. I'd like to think that Blizzard is smart enough to know that it has to reward people who take 'the road less traveled' and make these types of decisions.

'What do you mean by that?', you ask! I mean that if Blizzard gave warlocks the ability to use swords, then I'm quite sure that they're devising specialized weapons that will fit the mold, so to speak. After all, what good is being able to wield a sword when all the swords out there only boost STR or AGI, right? I guarantee you that somewhere along the line you'll see very caster-specific swords for those of us who take this path. By doing this, I also free up my second hand for a fetish, stein, or other stat-boosting item. Most other warlocks that I've seen use staves, which of course take both hands to wield. I just trained in swords last night, and bought a rather pricy one in the auction channel to use. However, I'm only 3/4 into level 17, and the sword requires level 18 to use. hehe. DOH! I'll let you know more about my experience as a sword-wielding 'Lock once I've had a chance to play with it.

One last thing to add before I go play for the evening: My travels have gone thusly:

Levels 1-7ish: Tirisfal Glades
Levels 8ish-12ish: Brill mostly
Levels 13ish-present: Silverpine, and I just recently moved to the Barrens, where I'm having a blast.

That's all I have time for today. I hope I've been able to convey my experiences well enough to give you all an idea of the Warlock class, and perhaps shed some light on other comings and goings of the incredible experience that is World of Warcraft along the way. Feel free to comment, ask questions, etc. And for you other beta testers, feel free to look me up on the regular beta server as Enshrouded. I've not ventured to the PvP server yet, but I will soon enough. It's time for a genocide of the Alliance races, and I'll be there to see it through. Hail the HORDE!
