World of Warcraft and what Blizzard gets what others just don't.
By Christopher Buecheler | Sept. 14, 2004

Like many gamers, I participated in the World of Warcraft stress test over the past week. After messing around with character generation for a bit, checking out the different races, I decided to go with an undead rogue. After getting my character set up and giving him a name, I jumped into the game ready to see whether Blizzard -- a company whose only previous multiplayer experiences are completely dissimilar to an MMORPG -- had a winner on its hands or not.

The first thing I noticed is that the graphics engine itself is not jaw-dropping. This is pretty standard for MMO games, since they can't exactly push DOOM 3 technology while also supporting thousands of players, but the technical aspects of Blizzard's engine have been surpassed even by some already established games like Asheron's Call 2.

The second thing I noticed? It doesn't matter.

The game is stunningly beautiful. It's not the textures, or the models, or the special effects, or the lighting ... it's all of those things. It's more than those things. World of Warcraft has the most cohesive art direction I've seen since, well, Warcraft 3. You could almost convince me that one guy was responsible for all the artwork in the game, though, of course, that's impossible.

That's all well and good, though, but it matters little if the game itself sucks. After all, "pretty but soulless" is practically a genre in game design, right? I could tick off about a half-million games from the past five years that fit right into that mold. Is World of Warcraft just a beautiful EverQuest clone, or does it bring something more to the table?

I played my character up almost to level 15 over the course of the week or so that I was in the stress test. I did many, many quests. I formed groups with friends and with people I'd never met before. I chatted in the game and surfed the forums. I was playing and having fun, sure, but I was also trying to gauge the game on a more critical level, mainly with this column in mind.

What I found was interesting. Does the game have the most complex quest system I've ever seen? Nope. Does it have the smartest NPCs and monsters? Nope. Does it have the most innovative social structures? The most original classes and skill systems? Trade skills that don't involve mindlessly and repetitively clicking on a button? Nope, nope, and nope.

But in the end, all of that falls by the wayside and gives way to something that you always, always find in Blizzard titles: polish. No, not the Eastern European culture ... the stuff you do to silver. Blizzard takes basic gameplay that people understand, and then it tweak it, shines it up, and add all of the little bits that make you laugh, or ask your friends if they've seen it yet, or think, "Hey, cool!"

Asheron's Call has portals that let you move rapidly from area to area. Some other titles have teleportation spells, or perhaps boats. World of Warcraft has Goblin Zeppelins, flying gryphons, and a ridiculously fun set of bat rides through woods and caves that I found myself taking just for the fun of it, from time to time.

The game is fun, in ways that most MMO's simply aren't. The experience hasn't been reduced (though it's not the most complex title out there, either). Instead, it's simply been streamlined. Cleaned up. Each aspect of the game has been (or is being -- it's still beta, remember) addressed to ensure that it's, well ... fun.

I can find things to complain about, if I try. The quests are almost exclusively either collection or "FedEx" quests (take something and run it somewhere else), though many are well disguised, and nearly all of them have fun backstory and interesting dialog. Like most MMO titles on the market, there are far too few choices for initial character customization. Hey Blizzard, if you need someone to just make tons of faces, hairstyles, and tattoos -- give me a yell!

There are bugs here and there, broken scripts, clipping issues, and at the moment a distinct lack of solo-able quests starting at around level 15 (at least for the undead). The trade skills are, as mentioned, still pretty much limited to clicking a button and waiting for your skill level to go up. I'd love to see an MMO where some sort of actual dexterity or puzzle-solving ability on the part of the user was necessary for trade skills. If someone can find a way to make them into a mini-game, you'll have a LOT more people being things other than warriors and magicians. At the moment (and probably in the final version), World of Warcraft isn't that game.

Some of these will be fixed. Some probably won't. What I know from the stress test is that whatever final form the game takes, Blizzard is going to make every effort to polish it up and make sure it shines. This -- and I wish more companies understood this fundamental truth -- can make up for a LOT of shortcomings. The game works, it's great to look at, and it's loaded with "the little things."

That's important to me, and important to a lot of other gamers. Blizzard's going to have a runaway success on its hands, a title whose popularity people will be quick to associate simply with the Warcraft name. The truth is that it has more to do with the quality that others associate with that name, than with the name itself.

Polish is what gives a game "soul" and helps it to be the type of title that people remember and talk about fondly, years later. Sort of like most of Blizzard's other games, even the ones it made before turning into an industry behemoth. Other companies would do well to understand this, and emulate it.

Oh, and if you think this whole column was an excuse for me to beg for invitation into the closed beta, you uh ... you might be right. Maybe. If you're from Blizzard, and want to extend such an invitation, or if you're not and just want to send me feedback about this article, then drop me a line.
