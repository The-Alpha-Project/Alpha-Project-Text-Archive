Article written by Mogrin

How exciting it was when the new push was released. Along with the new PvP-Server, balance changes, zone and instance additions, came a new 2GB client that thousands had to re-download, but boy was it ever worth the wait. I sat at home one day, exhausted, tired, and sat down to get in some playing time before bed. I try to connect to the server but it gives me an error, a “Wrong Version” error. First I get worried, that perhaps my version is corrupt and can’t download patches, but then I catch on and immediately stamp in www.worldofwarcraft.com and unsurprisingly enough there it was, titled “Patch Notes”.

I began downloading the new client, got rid of my old one and began to browse through the patch notes. There was boring/uninteresting balance changes to other classes (I play a warrior mainly) so I skipped through and just saw that they opened new higher level zones and some new instance dungeons, as well as lengthened the level cap to 45, adding in new plate-armor and mounts. Mounts were a big deal, especially since now it was a race to get one, and costing 100 gold, as well as 400 skillpoints and a character at level 40, mounts are rare in the first months of this new PvP realm.

As I went to sleep, I woke up excited. I raced to my computer, crusty eyed, tired and pretty cranky but I got real happy when I saw the “Finished” next to my bit-torrent. I installed World of WarCraft, and started my character, Bronzebeard (Dwarf Warrior) on the PvP server. Suffice to say you don’t see a big difference from the PvP and Beta server from levels 1-10. After that, you move onto other areas where Horde will sometimes try to raid because the guards are of lower level and it’s easier on them.

I got real excited when I saw raids forming in StormWind (the human’s major city) and even more excited when I was called upon to take care of Horde gankers camping in Westfall, killing people in a Creep-Town (A town with buildings, occupied by enemies) as soon as they got into battle. The feeling you get after sending home the opposition in bandages is indescribable until you actually do it. You might be asking; where does raiding finally come in? I know so far I have only touched on it, but to understand the entire cycle of how you progress to participate in raids, you must listen to the entire story.

Continuing on, as I hit level 14 I defended a small raid in Goldshire where a bunch of Rogues where going stealth and murdering our NPC’s. I got killed a few times, and the powerful Mages of StormWind came in invisibly, waited until the Rogues struck and then proceeded to nuke the living crap out of the rogues.

At level 15, sitting in Loch Modan I heard a call for people to help in the raid. There was no level cap, so I decided to try it out. I got together a group in Loch Modan who were going to the raid, and together we made our first journey through the much higher-level Wetlands, a zone intended for level 20+ characters which we had to run through to get to a place called Menethil Harbor, the harbor that took you to Auberdine, Darkshore (Night Elf Zone) or Theramore Isle (Nestled in Dustwallow Marsh, a Horde zone). My crew and I took a while, but we all arrived at the harbor after a few ghost runs and ported to Theramore Isle. We got together and met in Theramore, and would walk all the way into the Barrens and planned to hit the ever-popular city called the Cross-Roads. This was a pretty large city, located smack-dab in the middle of the Barrens and served as a big trading market (Largest of the Horde, I assume) as well as gave many quests to a variety of levels.

Once we all gathered in Theramore, our brave venture began. We came deep into the heart of Dustwallow Marsh, and despite getting pounded by super-high mobs, we endured. It was kind of naïve for us to think that we would have made it, as 2 level 45 undead mages came in on their nightmare mounts and smoked our raid while we were getting owned by a train of mobs. Everyone died. Our group broke up, with about 10 people still willing to try to raid.

We decided to travel to Auberdine, a Night Elven city off the coast of Darkshore and go through Ashenvale Forest, a zone with easier creeps. When we got to Auberdine, not 5 people showed up to our meeting place, and the raid collapsed. I was so disappointed, my first real raid and it failed.

However I strolled through town and found at least 5-6 quests. I abandoned my quests in Red Ridge and Loch Modan and decided to make Auberdine my new home, at least temporarily. WoW is so in-depth that something as simple as a raid turned into something quite complex with 40 people, and took me halfway around the world to a strange new land which I quickly adopted and began new and unique quests in. I really felt the greatness of this game as I realized what had happened. I quested all week in the strange and dark zone, leveled up and the next Sunday night, channel Raid erupted.

People were ready for the weekly Sunday Night Raid, and we began to gather in Auberdine once more, learning from our past mistakes. Old raiding buddies I met last week returned, 1-2 levels higher, as well as even more people and for the most part little-to-no raiders were under level 15. The majority was 20 and over. As about 20 of us gathered at the Inn, with at least 15-20 more on their way (some at level 30), I began to get quite excited. A knock on my door echoes through my hallway. I remark “Brb” and answer the door. A man in a blue mechanic-type outfit is standing in my doorway.

“Hello sir, there is a small electrical problem on your block. I ask you kindly to turn off all appliances such as computers, televisions and anything else you don’t want to suddenly cut the power off to, so we don’t short-circuit any power-supplies” he said kindly. I knew that the man didn’t control when or where he worked, but I couldn’t help but feel angry. I slowly said “gtg” and turned off my computer, and shut down my real chance at a raid.

Though I must say the experience so far has been so exciting and fun, I thirst for raiding now more than ever. Interestingly enough, before beta I planned not to go on a PvP server. Now I am more than counting on it, and so I advise all players who wish to have nothing to do with PvP-type play to try it once, and be in a raid. If they hate it, to each his/her own, but do not badmouth something you haven’t tried.
