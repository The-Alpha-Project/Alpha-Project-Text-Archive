There have been a few video games set in the Old West, but cowboys and gunslingers have been outnumbered by an endless parade of computerized aliens, wizards and soldiers of fortune. Red Dead Revolver is Rockstar Games' entry into the neglected world of westerns.

Red Dead Revolver's main appeal lies in its unusual setting. If you would rather pack a six-shooter than an Uzi, a laser gun or fireballs, you may find it to your liking, but the gameplay alone isn't enough to draw fence-sitters in.

The game, available for the PlayStation 2 and the Xbox, plays out in a series of chapters. The first chapter casts you as young Red, the taciturn hero of the game, facing down marauders on his family's homestead. Later chapters put you in the boots of Red as an adult, or cast you as one of a handful of side characters like Jack Swift, the English sharpshooter, and Shadow Wolf, the American Indian scout.

Outside of the Old West theme, the levels are pretty standard fare. You find keys, pick up healing items, face bosses and shoot lots and lots of people. The game's attempts at clever and unusual objectives -- like protecting a stagecoach full of explosives from a gang of desperadoes on horseback -- break up the routine bang-banging nicely, but aren't particularly innovative or memorable.

The nicest touch is the ability to put your back up against solid objects and peek around corners to shoot. This adds a nice bit of tactics to the game and makes it feel like a proper western. Good guys and bad jump out from behind rocks and corners to shoot, then duck back to safety.

The story is strictly linear and not particularly compelling. A lot of the themes, like the bounty hunter with a dark past and the unscrupulous land grabs, are straight from classic westerns. Others, like the death-dealing circus freak show and the geysers of blood that follow each shootout, aren't something you'd see in a Gary Cooper movie. In the end the plot is little more than watching someone in a cut scene explain who you're going to shoot, then going off and shooting them.

Graphically, the game tries to capture the feel of an old movie by inserting scratches and dust particles over unsaturated color schemes. The effect is cool for a few minutes, then annoying for a couple hours until you just don't notice it any more.

A central part of any shooter is the weapons selection. Whether you like the armaments available in this game depends on your interest in six-shooters. No room-clearing rocket launchers or rail guns here, just a selection of bad-ass revolvers, rifles and shotguns, and your choice of a knife or dynamite to throw. (There is a Gatling gun, too.)

The most innovative part of the game is also the most disappointing. Dueling mode is an admirable attempt to capture the feel of a western-style showdown, setting you up to slap leather against an assortment of gun-slinging enemies. Unfortunately, the controls are awkward and the slow-motion "bullet time" effect makes it feel more like a taffy pull than a gunfight.

The three multiplayer modes are nothing to shout about. Once everything's unlocked, you have a massive selection of characters and venues, but beyond that the options are sparse. The two arena-based multiplayer modes both revolve around collecting bounties by shooting your enemies, and grabbing giant playing cards for power-ups. Dedicated shooter fans will be disappointed by the lack of custom maps and the limited set of custom rules. They will be even more disappointed at having to play in split-screen mode, with no options for online play or linking consoles.

The third multiplayer mode is just a showdown with up to four players standing still and drawing guns on each other. It makes the already awkward dueling controls even more awkward by adding target-switching.

If you're a dedicated fan of westerns and video games, or if you're aching for a change from standard shooters, then Red Dead Revolver is definitely worth a rental, if not a purchase. On the other hand, if the thought of gaming in the Old West leaves you as cold as a sleeping sidewinder in Saskatoon, you'd best keep movin' on.
