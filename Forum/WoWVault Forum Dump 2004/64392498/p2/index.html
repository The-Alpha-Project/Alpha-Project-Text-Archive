As for the history of games and what they brought or how they impacted in North American MMORPG markets. (many many games not mentioned as they flat out failed to impact market what so ever. Liniage not mentioned as its impact is in Korea at the moment not North America)

Original NWN (on AOL): first graphical online persistant world game to grasp a wide variety of players imagination. Huge following (huge relation to actual population) for PvP (consentual) players. Max population was 200 for most of time and 500 for last year in release. Zoned PvP to avoid unconsentual PKing.

The Realms (Seirra): Grand Father version of what DAoC is today. First game to offer player housing. Zoned PvP to avoid unconsentual PKing.

Dark Sun Online : Definatly a step backwards from NWN and The Realms. Main draw was the popular Dark Sun AD&D world. Poor interface and combat. First MMORP game to have tasks or merchant style quests intensive. Zoned PvP to avoid unconsentual PKing.

Meridian 59: Terrible game but the birth of PKers for all intensive purposes. Succesful following for its time but never achieving the numbers maintained by NwN or The Realms in their day.

Utlima Online: The first MASSIVE multiplayer online role playing game. Contrary to popular myth, Ultima only truely created one aspect of gaming...trade crafts. It did how ever expand on all aspects previously created by its fore fathers. Houseing: Huge and still best over all to date. Crafting: set the market standard not to be rivaled for many years. PKing: Absolute unchecked chaos over shadowing any good aspects of game. Bugs: Quite possibly the most bugs of any release to date and after of any product. Population levels: Unheard of Population numbers for its time more then doubleing previous game population numbers.

NOTE: Unchecked PKing is the main and number one reason UO lost so many players when EQ started. Proving unchecked PKing does NOT sell the the main market of players. Unpunished exploiting and hacking became a very close 2nd as reason for leaving.

Double note: UO set the standard for the poor customer service found in todays online games.

EQ: Market standard for all MMORPG games to date. Definatly the most popular North American game ever released. Flag PvP system while fundamentally flawed was a huge huge huge improvement over the unchecked PKing of UO. Communication in game was superb. EQ had many faults but in the end they basically created a game that was fundamentally "better" then any aspect previously offered in any MMORPG other then crafting and Housing".

NOTE: EQ lowered the standard even more by limiting the Customer service hours of operation outside of game but not allowing in game "GMs" to have ability to fix problems.

AC: Not much really can be said about AC, It didnt raise or improve the bar over anything offered in EQ or UO. It lowered the bar in customer service (or lack of). About the only different aspect it offered was a changing in game storyline (even this idea was nothing new and had been used to basically same results in DSO).

AO: First real attempt into Sci Fi style MMORPG. Not a huge hit but first attempt at something outside of Fantasy.

DAoC: DAoC raised the bar again incorperating Massive PvE popularized in EQ with consentual area PvP originated in NwN on AOL and The Realms. Raised the bar in crafting from UO to modern times. First TRUE army warfare MMORPG created. Recreated houseing in MMORPG by reintroducing a seperate housing zone (as used originally in "The Realms", eliminating the lag issues during combat that accompanies UO housing to this day.

Note: Improved Customer service slightly from EQ and UO mostly through lip service rather then hands on helping.

Shadowbane: Attempted but ultimately failed attempt to reinvent PK style MMORPGs. Proving once and for all unchecked PKing is only popular to a small minority of players online.

Star Wars Online: Newest of the Star Wars products. Flawed game in keeping with most Star Wars related software games. Definatly raised the bar over AO sci-fi. Didnt really introduce anything new.

World of Warcraft: Its impact on market still pending.

All MMORPG's, by nature, have more things in common than they don't. Lets face it, this genre is full of conventions that make it what it is. Changing these staple fundamentals is dangerous to the potential of the game in question. That said, I am NOT expecting WoW to be dramatically different from any other MMORPG. What I AM expecting, is for Blizzard to refine the standard conventions into something more entertaining. Every single MMORPG has done something right, in some cases several somethings. The key to making a successful game is to get as many things right as possible. Rather than trying to make a fntasy-based MMORPG from all original concepts, I think Blizzard is looking to refine existing concepts and put their own unique twist on a few more. That is what I am hoping for, nothing more, nothing less.

Could WoW fail? Of course it could, it could turn out to be the most pathetic MMORPG ever produced. It could make Horizons, Shadowbane and AC2 into success stories. While all this is certainly possible, I don't think this will be the case. Blizzard is building on an already highly successful RTS franchise. They have a legion of Diablo and Starcraft fans that are most likely going to try the game out based upon prior experience with these games. The stage is set for a fairly large initial influx of players. Blizzard has made several addicting games already, if this one is even just as good as every other MMORPG (does not even have to be better) then it will be a rather large success. Will it dominate EQ, DAoC, SWG as well as EQ2 and Lineage 2? Time will tell. There is just as much chance it will as there are that it will fail. My money says it will at least be one of the Big 3.
