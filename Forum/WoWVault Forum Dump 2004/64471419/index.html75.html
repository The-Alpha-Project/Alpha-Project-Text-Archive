Okay, I know that everquest in it's current form is a boring, repetitive grind that is more work than fun, but this was not always the case. There are definite reasons for the games rise from obscurity to dominate the genre in western markets. There was a LOT that the game did right, especially in the early days. (Though each successive expansion detracted from it somewhat.) The game was a pioneer in the field, and every other game that sought to "dethrone" it has ultimately failed to a greater or lesser degree. I think a big reason for this is that the game's original vision was not to be "immersive" or "realistic", but to be fun to play. That’s why most the MMORPGs developed my EQ's competition are so dull in comparison - they are the product of developer masturbation. Think of the difference between a MUD and a hard core MUSH, which one is more fun? The one where you can be a warrior, cleric, or wizard and kill monsters all day, or the one that forces the majority of the player base to be a peasant, tailor, or blacksmith while only a elite few actually get to do something interesting?


The fact that the original everquest wasn't created solely for it's developer's self-gratification doesn't mean it gave it's player base everything it asked for. The game avoided sacrificing fun and challenge for the sake of BOTH the developers' and the players' whims. The result was a system that was based on the concept of "risk vs. reward" - a term that has virtually disappeared from MMORPG discussions these days. I think this notion is why people regard EQ's best features as it's biggest flaws. People want to have their cake and eat it to.


A major example of "risk vs. reward" in action was the corpse retrieval. The CR not only gave meaning to death, it also increased the element of risk in certain areas. If you wanted to hunt in a dungeon that had great loot and a great experience bonus, you had to risk a tough corpse retrieval - and possibly multiple deaths. If you played it safe and hunted in outdoor zones then you would level slower and receive less interesting loot. The concept of risk vs. reward created a huge element of excitement when hunting in dangerous zones that made it seem more than a mere grind. When there is actually something at stake, hunting becomes more of an adventure and less of a test to see who can put up with the more mind-numbing camping for the longest amount of time. Also, before the release of Kunark and zones that were filled with "see invis" mobs, CRs could actually be fun. Would I make it to the corpse before my invis fades? If I do, will it fade wile I'm dragging the corpse? And if it does, will I make it to the zone in time? For me, CRs in the Old World were some of the funest moments of the game.


Another thing people often criticize EQ about is the notion of class dependence. (The "holy trinity" of the Warrior, Enchanter, and Cleric are often cited as the most glaring examples.) The thing was, before Kunark came out, there really was no "holy Trinity". Druids and Shamans couldn't heal quite as well as clerics, but they could do well enough that any group could manage without one. Paladins and Shadowknights could tank almost as well as a warrior. Also, a group with a good monk, shadowknight or necromancer could do fine without an enchanter. The original everquest didn't have class dependence, it had ROLE dependence. Every member of a party had a different job to do - and this lead to teamwork and, dare I say it, strategy. While it's true that a party full of Warriors wasn't going to get very far in EQ, it was better than the current alternatives - where one person pulls a mob and everyone else just beats on it.


People also accuse EQ's class based system of forcing everyone to be the same, and this leads to a bunch of cookie cutter characters that are ultimately the same. I think the opposite is true - that EQ hast the MOST character diversity of any major MMORPG on the market today. One reason for EQ's diversity is that a player's race selection has little to no impact of his combat abilities. Therefore you don’t see a world where all the warrior are dwarves, all the paladins are humans, and all the mages are elves. Another reason for character diversity is that a class based system only allows for a certain degree of specialization. Everquest had 12 more or less viable classes, but an unlimited skill based system like Asheron's Call had three - an archer with some healing and buffing skills, a melee character with some buffing and healing skills, and a mage with some buffing and healing skills. The reason is because a character that specialized in doing only one thing was much stronger than a character spreads his skills out over a verity of areas.


I could go on, but the point is that so many of everquet's competitors failed because the refused to acknowledge the things in the game that worked. They claimed to get rid of the repetitive level grind by softening death penalties, but instead this made the grind more repetitive. The claimed to make more diverse characters by adding a skill based system, but instead created a system where a few flavor of the month characters were king. The claimed to make items more interesting by adding randomly generated loot, but instead created loot that was essentially the same as all the other loot, but with different numbers. They promised to create more immersive worlds by adding things like player-run economies, but instead created worlds that were dull and ultimately un-engaging.


I hope WoW doesn't continue this trend.
