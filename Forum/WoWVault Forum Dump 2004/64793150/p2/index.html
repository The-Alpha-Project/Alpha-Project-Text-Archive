Date Posted: 2/23/04 4:40pm Subject: RE: Why go the Level Route?
The thing I dislike most about skill systems is how they basically turn into a "one-class" system, as someone already mentioned. People figure out the one or two most optimal skill sets, and then that is what everyone plays. Well-defined roles disappear, and those types of games basically turn into soloer games, as everyone works to have the most optimum set of skills to compete.


I like rigid, class-based games, where I get to choose my character profession from the beginning (Paladin, Shadowknight, Cleric, Shaman, etc). I love games that have a tightly defined class system and those classes have well defined roles that are designed to work together. Those systems seem to promote the most grouping and cooperative play.

I also find them very fun - because I know what my character *is*, and I know what my role is. It also creates a lot less stress, for me as a player, because I don't have to worry about how to build my character, if I'll be gimped, or have to untrain skills, and whatnot.

Rigid classes also seem, to me, to provide a lot of replayability. I look back on EQ and think of all the secondary characters I made because I wanted to try a different class, and see how they worked, and how the playstyle was. It was fun to have diverse and different characters, so I could play whatever struck me that moment. Maybe I wanted to sit back and relax that evening, so I play a support role like a cleric or druid. Maybe I want to be puller, so I play my Shadowknight or a monk. Maybe I want to solo, so I play my necro...

Plus, there's something cool about being able to just look at someone else's avatar and KNOW what they are, like a dark elf necro, or a troll shaman. I mean.. you saw these characters and you knew what they were by the way they dressed. You could tell a ranger from a warrior just by looking.


Bottom Line - I like classes, and levels. Glad WoW has them =)

There was a great deal of freedom as to how your character would grow. what would have made UO great would have been a system of mathmatical curves for various skillsets that said ) that if Skillset X is at level 100 then all other sets can only ever get as high as 50... as an example:

Skillset X contains all Melee combat specializations for various weapon/combat styles, and Skillset Y contains all magic, including healing, buffs, and various forms of offensive spell casting.

Timmy the Crusher of Souls has just spent all his most recently skill points in maxing out 2 specific skills, one is in his favorite weapon, the human head swung by the hair, and the other is in "buttwhoopin' '' fighting style, which allows for dual wielding and has special moves that involve attacks against an enemies rump, but doesn't allow the use of shields or armor heavier than light chain. Both skills are in skillset X, and by maxing out one weapon and one fighting style, Timmy has hit the 100 point bracket for melee combat.

After seriously whomping a troupe of elven girlscouts that throw poisoned cookies at you, Timmy has earned himself another 10 skill points.( they used to be worth more, but the TOTAL number of skill points a player has will always be a factor in determining how much a player gets for accomplishing anything) He decides as the crusher of souls, it's important to be able to cast some pretty evil looking spells from time to time. When Timmy goes to pump all 15 points into "skull magic" however, he runs into a little problem... He's already put 20 points into skull magic (just enough to hurl one flaming skull at his foes, and grant him the abbility to avoid combat with low level undead beasties if he chooses) and 25 points into magic resistance(the only skill that offers blanket protection from any incoming spell-nasties). after putting 5 more points into skull magic, the game tells Timmy he's reached the cap, and that if he wants to continue pumping points into skull magic, he's going to have to either lower the total number of points he's put into his highest skillset, melee combat, to the next lowest bracket which is at 84 points, or drop his points in magic resistance (anything under 15 makes you an obvious target for equal-level enemy wizards, and even at 25 a high level caster can still knock Timmy stupid if he isn't crafty in his aproach).

If he chooses to drop those 16 points, he doesn't get them back (to stop people from power-leveling as a fighter then switching all their points to magic to be a top level wizard), but he can then raise his total devotion to magic up to 60.

OH NO!!!!! trade 16 points for 10? well, it's a way to balance things out to avoid making "jack of all trades" characters overly powerful.... There would always be a cost for not choosing to be REALLY good at anything, hence the concept of a curve instead of a straight line graph.

This kind of system can continue down with each kind of skillset having lower caps... yes players can choose how to develop their characters into individuals, but there will always be limits to how powerful you can be, and after you've reached the max any new points can only be spent "fine-tuning" your skill spread to be the way you like it.

These examples were pulled off the top of my head so obviously there are balance issues that a programmer would have to look at and testers would have to play-through, but the basid idea is there. Skill points are earned like exp in a level based system, but without the rigid levels that some people hate. Characters can be customized, but overly powerful combinations are largely ruled out as people will always have to choose to either be Godly at one thing, or good at several.

Date Posted: 2/23/04 8:34pm Subject: RE: Why go the Level Route?
Any system is fine as long as it's fun and balanced. However, my preference is the freer skill system because it doesn't focus on combat prowess.

A major obstacle with any system is just how much the designers do about balance. It's easier to balance level-based system because it's always combat-centric. They just slap on a different system for trade skills. What usually happens is that crafters and non-combatants get nowhere in this type of game without combat skills that support the group. Instances like a reporter who has no combat skills will not be able to visit the toughest places because of level restrictions; or maybe you want to bring along a blacksmith to repair your gear on the battlefield but he doesn't have the level requirement to enter the zone.

In my ideal skill system, all skills are equally important and viable, and they have intricate relationships. Learning one skill may benefit another similar skill but hamper another unrelated skill. In simple terms, skills belong to categories. Using a skill in one category improve your chances on other skills in the same category. Skills in different categories may show antagonistic effects. Heavy armor slows down your casting time, for instance; or training magic-related skills earns you more mana points to use, so if you want a lot of mana points, you want to train a lot of magic skills and give up other skills. Even non-combatants can have great success in the game: maybe not as killers, but they can become great merchants and craftsmen without killing ever. They can go anywhere with an escort because places don't have arbitrary level restrictions.

A close example would be AC2 where skills are divided into melee, missile, and magic. To get the best skills, you have to train the lower skills in the same category. You have the freedom to choose any skill from any skill tree, if you have enough credits, thus creating a hybrid class. Of course, generally, skill systems are even less restrictive. Something along the lines of GURPS complete with prerequisites, default skill levels (because you know a similar skill), etc., things that encourage focusing on one or two line of skills. It should be easier and faster to raise related skills. Unrelated skills may have inverse effect on your performance in another skill, thus mitigating the desire to make tank mages.

The toughest part of designing this kind of system is defining the types of relationships among all the skills. In general, weapon skills encourage physical combat, magical skills make one a better spellcaster, and craftsmen can do their own thing at peace and still be able to enjoy a major portion of the game. Other factors to consider are time investment, character attributes, and other game dynamics (eg, physics, speed, item attributes including weight and size, etc), to help balance the skills. A more complex system should be harder to abuse. There will always be min-maxing, but there won't be a single dominant template. You can still mix skills, however, there will be some restrictions for realism and for balance's sake. The final result is that you move away from hack-and-slash to a more well-rounded online society. It's not a game just for those who like mind-numbing gore-fests; people who like peaceful activities can also come to be productive, to socialize and to explore.

I prefer a class system but with a ton of variety and choices within he class. When I think of a class system I see there being at least three different groups of skills/spells. First group are skills/spells that your class gains automatically. These are basic class defining abilities like the ability to wear plate armor, or certain spells like heal for a priest. Then there are skills/spells you can choose to develop that only your class can choose. Then there are the abilities that most anyone can choose to develop.

Now there may be some choices to make and different pools of skill/spell/proficiency points to spend on these various groups.

Now certain class specific abilities may be so crucial that they essentially become class defining but that just happens.

Spells could evolve. Example, that bolt spell can over time increase range, increase accuracy, increase damage, increase AE component, increase dot component, and decrease cast time. A really cool system would allow you to customize your spells. Maybe even having a customizing ability to develop. Allows flexibility and uniqueness. Granted balancing is hard but it can change over time.

IMO, it's not levels or skills but the feeling of accomplishment. WOW I raised my sword skill 30 points today! WOW I gained 8 levels at that new hunting spot. People need to readily see the rewards for their actions or it is turned into a grind. Strictly leveling-wise, I MUCH more enjoyed going from 1-40 in AC than 40-80. Why? I saw less improvement going 40-80 for the amount of time I put in than going 1-40. Constantly seeing the swirling mists around me let me feel like I got something done. I don't have a problem with leveling or skill-based systems. I don't have a problem with getting into the upper middle level/skill ranges. The problems come when the game I play puts to much emphasis on the HIGHEST level or the HIGHEST skill. The fact that higher level character are almost assured victory strictly because they are higher level. The fact that I can only weild that sword with a skill of EXACTLY 325 or more. Then I feel like I HAVE to keep pushing past what I'm currently at in order to stay competative when I know I won't enjoy it as much.
