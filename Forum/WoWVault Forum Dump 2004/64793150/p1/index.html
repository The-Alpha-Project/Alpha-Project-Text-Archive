Date Posted: 2/23/04 1:33pm Subject: RE: Why go the Level Route?
There are issues with skill based systems too. The main one that people complain about got it's name from UO: the tank mage.

It just comes down to the fact that a small subset of all the skills are considered very valuable. If you allowed all players to choose any skill they wanted, a large number of them would choose a very similar skill set. This leads to many characters being 'cookie-cutter'. Further, players who don't have the optimal skill set are 'gimped'.

Free pick skill systems are harder to balance. Since any player can have any combination of skills, they have to alot testing time for all possible combinations or risk being surpised (wow, GM fishing and GM terrain navigation together let you make SICK loot).

It is harder for them to provide challenging PvE content. If there is no solid definition of 'mid-level' then it is hard to make 'mid-level' content. With a skill based system t is far more likely to have gaps in content.

I have no problems with a skill based system, but I can understand why devs like level based stuff.

Date Posted: 2/23/04 3:35pm Subject: RE: Why go the Level Route?
Well, in my experience Level systems are simply a lot more fun than skill based systems for many reasons.

In skill based systems specialization is a huge problem and you often end up with characters that can only do one or two things. Playing these types of characters gets really old really fast. In level-based games this problem is avoided, as it makes it imposable for a class to overspecialize.

Nerfs are also disastrous in skill based systems, as they are often directed at characters that specialize in an overpowered skill. In the wake of such a nerf, a character that was designed to exploit such a skill is often cut down to size, but a non-exploitive character that relied on the nerfed skill can be end up completely gimped.

Likewise, underpowered skills are often completely ignored by the development staff. Often templates that seem viable result in unplayable characters. This creates a huge dependence on fan pages for character creation, as in many cases one MUST borrow a template just to create a useful character. (Ala AC1, where only a very small set of character templates were ever able to make it to the higher levels.)

Lastly group based play suffers greatly from skill-based systems. If all classes have a way to deal damage, mitigate damage, and heal damage then there is no reason for players to group. And if they do decide to group, the experience is not really changed. Everyone just ends up mindlessly whacking a mob whenever it spawns. (ala UO)

Open skill systems are valid, just not my cup of tea. They have a tendancy to make for a more solo kind of game, since you are likely to have all the skills you think you need to play.

I don't like them because I feel the violate 'The Contract'. I feel there is an unspoken contract between a game designer and the player. The contract is that if the designers create a type of play in the game, it will be as valid as any other type of play

So, if you can be good or evil, then both good and evil will be equally supported. If they set it up so that good had a bunch of advantages and evil was always destined to lose (as players) they wouldn't be fun or balanced.

Complaints about class balance are players feeling this unspoken contract being broken (not all classes are equally supported).

So, open skill systems that let players create broken characters that cannot play the game, is a violation of this contract. That's why I don't like em. Your milage may vary.

Hob
