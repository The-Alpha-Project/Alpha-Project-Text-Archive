Date Posted: 11/10 7:58am Subject: RE: Whats the purpose of the elite tag on mobs?
To reduce it to functional terms:

Similarities - level 17 elite has, for the sake of arguement, the same hit points and hit power of a level 25 normal mob.

Differences - if you are level 17, you can hit and damage a 17 elite mob. A lvl 25 mob would evade and resist the shitzors out of you, and utterly pwnzor you.

So, hits and power: same. Chance to hit: different. That's the main difference.

If they did it any other way, then groups could tackle mobs 5-10 levels their surperior, and that is not what Blizzard has designed for the game. Yes, it may be what other games were like, but not this one.

Date Posted: 11/10 8:29am Subject: RE: Whats the purpose of the elite tag on mobs?
Lotta people here saying all the same thing. Hopefully it's clear now.

Why build it this way? WoW is built to be handled by the most common type of players: solo'ers and small groupers, where small means 2-4 players. The vast majority of MMORPG players play with their few close RL/online friends, or alone, when the game does not otherwise force larger groups. This is most players' comfort zone. WoW was designed to give players an environment where the content can be explored 90% by solo/small group play. Only 10% of the game requires full 5-member groups, and "elite" mobs fall into that 10%.

The combat system is designed to keep you fighting mobs that are 2-3 levels +/- of your level. Outside that range, the mobs are either worthless (too low) or insanely impossible to harm (too high). This is on purpose. If higher level mobs, say 5-10 levels above, were doable with just a sufficient number of much lower players, then that's how everyone would play the game. By restricting survivable combat to a 2-3 lvl +/- range, you give people the opportunity to enjoy the game at a solo/small group pace, instead of giving them the perception that they *need* a large group to take on mobs that are *much* higher level in order to level, and anything less is just wasting time. I love this design, and about 90% of the MMORPG community will also. The 10% who looooooooooove the idea of grouping with 8-50 people to accomplish a common goal can either be sated by the limited amount of such content in WoW, or move on. Either way, WoW will have cornered the market on the majority of players, and will have a game that both works better than any previous (meaning lacking bugs and insane balancing issues) and also makes more money than any previous. The *only* game in MMORPG history to do this as well was UO, and no one but the ooooooooooldies play that game because of the out-of-date graphical design/engine.

This 90/10 split I refer to isn't well represented on game boards, where the ratio is close to 50/50 (those who prefer solo/small group to those who prefer 8-50 man groups). So as long as you keep that in mind when reading the boards, that very many more people here desire content that WoW offers only in limited quantities, then you'll be alright when taking in the opinions and advice offered here.
