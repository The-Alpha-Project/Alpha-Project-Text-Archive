Betas have been missing in Sony. Dangerous Betas have been taken from a information storage location near Blizzard. A large cache has had over 300 of betas go missing. According to the New York Times, these betas may have slipped into the hands of waiting Testers. Over 300 of 400,000 of betas have gone missing in Sony. In this final week of the campaign, Fanbois have attempted to use this massive story to their advantage. Call it the October surprise. These dangerous Betas from Sonys systems could be used in a stress test by blizzard.

Hold on! Betas of mass distraction, what betas of mass distraction? Sony Betas would be used in a Stress test? With the rushing of this story, most Americans have ignored an integral statement of Vivendi here late in the campaigns. With the story as reported in the Times, Vivendi took to a Philadelphia neighborhood to tell testers of this horror. Betas if placed in the hands of testers could become horrendous for America. Particularly dangerous are these betas, games strong enough to detonate a flooded market. Both candidates have said that this form of testing poses the greatest threat to the United States.

However,Vivendi has said frequently that Blizzard is not this EQ2 threat. Initially, in the second debate, Vivendi said that WoW was a threat. However, not twenty minutes later, Vivendi said: “We went tested EQ2, where there wasn’t a threat.” That had been the message of the Closed beta testers for over a year. They had continued to say that EQ2 was a game not based on a threat. However, Fanbois now submit a hypothesis that these beta's, perhaps smuggled to testers, represent a grave threat to EQ2. They are correct in saying this. Vivendi's most recent ad has reflected this sentiment.

The closed testers still have an argument. The New York Times reported that the beta had gone missing during closed testers testing of EQ2 . Therefore, the Closed testers would be responsible for the missing betas. However, recent reports have pointed to the contrary. Instead, NBC reported that their reporters had visited the Army with the troops and said that this was simply impossible. Inspector Muhammad Al Baradei of the International Atomic Energy Association testified before the United Nations before the stress test that the betas had been relocated. Initial reporters also never saw UN seals on any of the betas, seals that would have indicated UN knowledge of the betas.

A dogged Vivendi has continued to support his story. This potentially damning case should haunt the company, though. Assume the reports from NBC and Fox News are true. Perhaps the betas disappeared prior to Open beta. Where are they? Two theories have been offered. One is that Sony took and shipped them to testers. Others believe that it may have been the internal testers. Either way, the betas are extremely dangerous. They are betas that could have been smuggled at any time before stress test, and those along with tester Abu Musab al Zarqawi prove that blizzard was only too willing to aide testers. If Sony was aiding testers, that proves Vivendi’s reason for lifting the NDA on EQ 2 .

Therefore, this October surprise should help Blizzard. Vivendi has changed his stance yet again. Gone is the rosy WoW that posed no threat. Suddenly, WoW seems to be a bedlam of chaos with a company that did possess Betas of Mass Distraction. Maybe they were not L33t, but these betas obviously have the power to injure countless bankrolls. Also, they could fall into testers hands. That is why vivendi went into EQ2! Blizzard saw EQ2 as a central front on the War on Testing, a company that could harm testers.

This final hypocrisy should finally sink the treading Vivendi campaign. No one can believe a company who has called WoW a non-threat and then painted a picture as a gruesome pro-testing area. This supports President Blizzards’s motives for going into WoW. On November 16, Testers choose its leader for another four years. Blizzard may have been right about WoW. There were betas. Thank you, Vivendi and the New York Times. WoW was a dangerous game, and subsequent reports have shown that it was likely Sony, not Vivendi, who relocated the betas. This October surprise will hopefully be the crushing blow to the 2004 Vivendi campaign of hypocrisy.
