Actually PArAhAwK, this is pretty normal everyday reading vocabulary. None of the words I've used are even remotely close to being evanescent. In fact, I'll wager most of them you'll find in Baron's SAT book for highschool students to study for the verbal exam. When it comes down to it the english language, while being a very broad language in terms of its ability to describe a vast amout different information effectively tends to be a bit vapid when one is looking for word polymorphism. One could blame this on the fact current thesauruses tend to limit themselves in terms of the older english words they include. For instance, I've seen some current thesaurses leave out the word ire as a synonym for anger; and ire has only been part of english since Shakespeare introduced it.

Personally, I think it has more to do with a basic paradigm shift in how people view vocabulary. It is very seldom that we see completely new words introduced into the english language. Rather the trend is to overload the definition of existing words, using their context to define meaning in each particular instance. While I'm sure there are good points to overloading definitions, at least there are a few in programing languages, it tends two precipitate two things I don't find very enticing. The first being that from constantly overloading current terms and using them in place of words with the actual meaning we desire, we are reducing the descriptive spectrum of the english language since as I've stated thesauruses tend to drop little used words. The second issue I have comes as a result of the first. By establishing this "genetic drift" for words, we lose some of the elegant attractiveness of the english language. Take for example the word ephemeral which means about the same thing as quick. Which one sounds better? Which one actually sounds like its disappearing as its being spoken? Thats the type of effect I'm talking about as people focus on having ninety thousand different ways to use only the word dude to express themselves.
