185. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/17/2003 10:54:44 PM GMTST
This is something I originally wrote for TheSuqs thread, but that thread died. It was written in reponse to TheSuqs post outlining what drives a successful economy system. The point of my post is that rules for a MMORPG-economy are the same, but purpose is different, so it should be formed after different ideals (a "fair" and functioning system isnt the ideal, a functioning economy in purpose of entertainment is the ideal).

"

Second, about the economy. Many of the points you make are very valid, but they are formed around the ideals set by the real world economy. Economy in MMORPGs work essentially the same way, but they do so with a different purpose, namely the entertainment of the players. That is why the logic used to form functioning real-life economies cant be applied to MMORPG-economies.

In the real economy, the individual is rewarded for either hard work, exploiting the system or making smart moves (those two are in many cases the same, question of definition...). If you are after economic wealth, this could be concidered "fair". In a MMORPG, you dont really want the game to be "fair", because the reward you are after is not economic wealth, it is entertainment. Many of the points you made in your post would indeed create a good ecnomy on paper, but it would be too "fair", since those who dont powergame, either by lack of interest or by lack of time, will face an economy that is too harsh for them to be able to enjoy the game fully.

This balance, the balance between the casual gamer and the powergamer, is in my opinon one of the most important parts in making a succesful game. You have to leave room for both, and not let the actions of one group of players affect individual players too much. The problems arise due to the fact that the powergamers get a large part of their entertainment from in-game success, unlike the casual gamer.

In my opinion, the economies in many MMORPGs fail because they are built with the wrong purpose. They are built around real life economy systems, and then adjusted to give all players a good chance at having a good time. Real life economy is a stranger to the concept of entertainment, and trying to add entertainment to it will cause some extreme behaviours of the economy system, leading to an economy that is non-functional in the long run. When Blizzard designs WoW economy, I really hope that they leave the common ideals of "functional economy" behind in favour of a system that promotes maximum fun for all players, from the most casual to the most extreme. This does not mean abandoning economy or the rewards of wealth, it means designing the system with a different ideal, understanding that the relationship between in-game success (in-game wealth) and entertainment is different for different groups of players.

The economy system designed for the game will likely be extremely complex, and will require a lot of thought to work well. As you said it is very important that the economy is working correctly before the game ships, correcting it post-release will take 10x the effort of getting it right in the fist palce, if it even is possible at all.

189. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/18/2003 2:30:51 AM GMTST
Hi, folks.

Hey, I'm glad my information dump was able to breathe some new life into this interesting and important discussion. Let me make some comments of my own:

1. Size of bank: I realize that 20 bags sounds like a HUGE amount of space, but the three things that call that into question in my mind are stack sizes, consumable crafted items, and crafter populations. Specifically, I've played several MMORPGs before, and finding a crafter in a time of need was often an exercise in frustration and/or futility. As a result, when you DO find a crafter that can make you items you need, you tend to over-buy as insurance against your next "crafter drought." Given that stack sizes cap out at about 20 in WoW (at least so far), that might mean that you could buy an entire BACKPACK worth of leather patches or whetstones when you find a master crafter online. As the dev described it, each piece of armor you have, plus your weapon, could be imbued with temporary magic from these consumable crafter pieces. That means that if you hit all your gear with a big AC bonus, you might tear through 8-10 patches each half hour, burning an entire stack of bonuses for every hour of adventuring. I know if I were a warrior, I'd have a backpack full of bonuses everywhere I went, just in case. If we add up all these bonuses: enchantments, whetstones, leather patches, foods, beverages, ammunition, potions, poisons, and who-knows-what, I'm sure backpack space will be in low supply after a few dozen levels, and the problem will likely remain for your entire gaming life.

2. PC vs. NPC economy: let me start by saying that I agree that there are two different economies, and that fixing one does not necessarily fix the other. That having been said, players will interact with both, and do so with the same currencies (gold, dropped items, etc). Inevitably, drops will fall into a few distinct categories: "cash items," "adventuring items," and "tradeskill items." By adjusting the vendor price of items, you might bump some items out of the second or third category and into the first, or vice versa, but adjusting prices should not be the primary method of controlling the economy. If the flow of currency from NPCs to PCs and back to NPCs is smooth enough, gold will neither flow steadily into or out of the players' hands, and the relative value of gold should remain fairly constant. While adjusting prices WILL affect the rate of flow, it will ALSO affect the wealth of all players, often to the detriment of players who were saving up for an upgrade. Furthermore, this will punish casual players severely -- if the game has 5% inflation per week, a casual player may find his real wealth (in purchasing-power terms) steadily falling despite attempts to save! This marks another fundamental departure between games and real life: while we're always in the real world, gamers can come and go as they please. The player who only adventures 5 hours a week and the player who adventures 60 hours per week will both be affected by any "macroeconomic" decisions applied to the game.

Personally, I think the most appropriate way to adjust the flow of funds into and out of the hands of players is by tweaking mob drop rates and ensuring the persistence of mandatory money sinks. After Gen Con, I'm FAR more confident that Blizzard is thinking about these issues. Hopefully, the beta will be long enough and thorough enough to sufficiently test "end game stability" for the economy.

Here's to the future. =)

198. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/21/2003 9:24:21 AM GMTST
Bag Theory

We have received quite a lot of new information about how the economy in WoW will work as a result of GenCon and also the addition of the Dungeons page to the WoW website. In particular we have received a lot of additional information about item storage limitations and the “bag” system of item storage in WoW.

TehSuq, myself and many others have expressed concerns about the ability of Bliz to move item wealth out of the player economy in WoW. So far we can see that item wealth will move into the player economy through mob drops, gathering and crafting, NPC vendor purchases, quest rewards, and possibly also rewards for arena combat and factional PvP achievements.

Item wealth will move out of the player economy through use of consumable items (like food, potions, whetstones, armour patches etc), equipping of soul bound items (“binds when equipped” items become non-transferable), sale of items to NPC vendors, use of ingredients in crafting and spellcasting, dropping of items (possibly through player death, or simply because a character can’t carry any more items), character deletion etc.

Previously in this thread we have discussed how it will be desirable for Bliz to set targets for player held item wealth (IES + IEW) and to monitor item wealth in the player economy and formulate tools to move actual item wealth towards target levels on a server. It is in support of this theory of setting and achieving item wealth targets that I want to introduce a new element to our economic model – I call it Bag Theory.

Obviously bags are going to play an important part in determining the quantity of items that players on a server will be able to store at any given time. A character will have 5 “bag slots” to equip with bags to carry items around in, and also an additional 5-10 bag slots in their bank account (depending on how rich the character is and whether they purchase additional slots) to store items in.

Now, in the long run Bliz will be able to influence total player held item wealth by playing around with all of the factors mentioned above which cause item wealth to flow into (and out of) the player economy. However, in the short run how can Bliz deal quickly and effectively with discrepancies between actual player held item wealth on a server and targets for player held item wealth?

My answer is Bag Theory. We know that some bags will have the capacity to store far greater quantities of items than other bags. We also know that Bliz intends to make it quite difficult for players to possess the best quality bags. Although it hasn’t been mentioned anywhere yet, I assume that good quality bags (like good quality items) will be “soul bound” and will “bind when equipped”, so they don’t get passed around from character to character in the player economy indefinitely.

A player with 10 or 15 really good quality bags may be able to store 5 times as many items (or more) than a player with 10 or 15 average quality bags. What type of bags players are able to get their hands on will therefore have a very significant effect on the amount of item wealth that is held by players at any point in time. So if we can come up with a mechanism to directly and quickly effect the quality of bags players have access to, we will be able to directly influence (IES + IEW).

How will players get their hands on good quality bags? Some may come as quest rewards or MOB drops, but I suspect most good quality bags will come from NPC vendor purchases and from crafting. So, if actual player held item wealth moves above the (IES + IEW) target on a server, I suggest that the following events take place automatically to combat the problem in the short term.

Firstly NPC vendor prices for bags will increase across the server by a percentage. The percentage increase will depend on how far actual player held item wealth has moved above the target level. For example

PIWmod = (IEPc - (IES + IEW))/(IES + IEW) x 150

where PIWmod = Player held Item Wealth Modifier (percentage)
where (IES + IEW) = target for total player held item wealth (previously defined)
where IEPc = total Current value of Items and Equipment held by Players

So we can see that according to this formula, if total current item wealth in the player economy on a server was calculated at 110,000 gold and the target for player held item wealth on that server was 100,000 gold, then the Player Item Wealth modifier (PIWmod) would be 15% and all NPC vendor bag prices would rise by 15% across the server without any Bliz intervention.

This is the beginning of a solution. However, the immediate result will be that purchase of bags will move out of the NPC sector and into the crafting sector of the economy. Crafters who produce bags will find that the demand for their goods has increased, and will therefore need to produce more bags and/or raise their prices. Therefore we now need to do something about the supply of good quality bags from crafting.

Crafting of good quality bags will require a recipe, an appropriate “crafting skill level” for performing the recipe, and ingredients. Players will already hold recipes and will have already developed their bag crafting skills, so the short term factor that we can play with to reduce the quantity of bags produced through crafting is the supply of bag crafting ingredients. I therefore propose that we tag all ingredients used in the crafting of bags with a B tag.

How will crafters get their hands on bag crafting ingredients? Well, some will come from gathering, some will come from MOB drops and some will come from NPC vendor purchase. I therefore propose that at the same time as NPC bag prices increase by PIWmod (15% in this example) Bliz also automatically increases the price of all B-tagged ingredients sold by NPC vendors by the same percentage. In addition to this B-tagged ingredients that drop from MOB kills should drop 15% less frequently (work PIWmod into the MOB drop formula for mobs that carry B-tagged ingredients).

Finally gathering of B-tagged ingredients by the use of gathering skills should become 15% more time consuming as well (work PIWmod into the formula for resource gathering for B-tagged ingredients). And that is the end of my Bag Theory solution for fixing discrepancies between “target” and “actual” player held item wealth levels in the short term. The beauty of this system (as I see it) is that it provides a (relatively) quick fix, and everything is taken care of without any intervention from Bliz staff by the incorporation of PIWmod into vendor pricing formulas, MOB drop formulas and resource gathering formulas.

This system will work just as well (but in reverse) if a situation arose where actual player held item wealth fell significantly below the Bliz server target. Please note that I am not proposing “Bag Theory” as a long-term solution to item wealth distribution problems. Bliz will still need to keep track of the movement of items into and out of the player economy through all the avenues described above and formulate long term item movement objectives and tools. Rather I see Bag Theory as a useful automatic short term adjustment tool, like loot pools, to keep the WoW economy (more or less) under control.

What do you think?
