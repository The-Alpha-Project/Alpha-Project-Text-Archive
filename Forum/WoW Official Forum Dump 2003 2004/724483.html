23. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/7/2003 1:05:17 PM GMTST
Macromanagement of the economy will involve measuring how much gold there is available to players in the world in total at any given time, and adjusting that amount by various means to keep it roughly constant on a per capita (per player) basis.

Tools will include adjusting average value of monster kill drops, average prices of items for sale in NPC shops, prices of services offered in the world (like ship transport, griffin taxi rides) etc. This is "macro" stuff.

Micromanagement of the economy will involve checking to see if wealth is being concentrated in particular areas of the world, or particular areas of the economy, and if so why. For example if the elves are getting incredibly rich through exploitation of some unique overpriced resource in Kalimdor.

Micromanagement can also look at concentration of wealth in an individual or character type or guild. e.g. If some guild is storing an amazing amount of gold in their guild hall and not spending it, a game master might decide to send in an NPC thief to take some of the gold.

The guild could then be given the opportunity to go on a quest to try to retreive the gold. They may not get all the gold back, but they might get other quest rewards which are pretty good so they dont feel ripped off. This is called forced spending.

So, thats what I mean by micro tools and macro tools for economic management.

39. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/10/2003 6:51:46 AM GMTST
I've started a post about player controlled "guild shops" in WoW at http://www.battle.net/forums/wow/thread.aspx?FN=wow-general&T=778189&P=1&ReplyCount=11#post778189 and I wanted to talk about the economic implications of this, without making the other post too boring.. :P

Player controlled shops are not new and have been implemented in other online games with varying levels of success. The idea is that if you produce stuff with neutral trade skills you dont want to have to run all over the place saying "Who wants to buy a nice pair of crafted boots? Who wants to buy a nice new sword? Who wants to buy some dragon strength potions?" etc.

You don't want to sell all your stuff to NPCs either, because they will underpay you, the stuff will disappear from the game world and it just isnt as fun. So, if you have a player controlled shop (like a guild shop) you can put your stuff there and let it sell itself, even if you are logged-off.

Guild shops really stimulate the "player to player" economy, making it much easier for players to sell and exchange goods between themselves without going through the NPC sector. I can see that some people will object to this idea on the basis that, because it moves sales and purchases out of the NPC sector, it will make it harder for Bliz to manage the WoW economy. The truth is, by enabling guild controlled shops, Bliz will actually gain access to a whole range of new economic indicators and control mechanisms.

If players can only trade with each other on a one-to-one basis it will be very hard to track movement of items and prices paid for items in player exchanges. For example if a number of items are exchanged at once Bliz wont know how much each item is worth to the purchaser, wont know how much factors like friendship are effecting the exchange, and in general it will be very hard to monitor and understand these exchanges.

If player to player exchanges are done mainly through guild shops on the other hand, Bliz will have easy access to information such as how valuable players believe items really are. Players will buy and sell items for what they think they are worth in the game world, and if the prices in guild shops are significantly different from prices Bliz puts on the same goods in the NPC sector then Bliz will know they are overpricing (or underpricing) certain goods.

Bliz will also have easy access to information on the amount of goods being produced and sold using neutral skills. If potions sales are booming and food sales are slumped in the player market Bliz will be able to take action, e.g. make certain herbs harder to find, increase the amount of herbs needed to make a potion, make it easier to gather food resources, require less raw food to cook things etc.

This way demand for neutral skills use can be brought back into balance. Bliz can check things like how many blacksmiths there are in the economy and how much income is being generated through sale of metal armour and weapons, how many tailors there are and how much income is being generated through sale of clothing, etc. With this additional economic information a lot of guess work will be taken out of the task of fine-tuning of the WoW economy.

Furthermore the player market will not really be "out of control" for Bliz. The reason is that a guild will pay a fee for the priviledge of keeping a guild shop, and will pay a wage to their chosen NPC shopkeeper. If the "player to player" economy is booming and the NPC economy is in a slump Bliz can raise the wage demends of these NPC shopkeepers.

Now less profitable guild shops will close down, prices will rise in the player market, the player to player economic activity will be "dampened" and consumption will move back to the NPC sector. On the other hand, if the player market is in a slump and no-one is using their neutral trade skills Bliz can lower NPC shopkeeper wage demands, making it possible for more guild shops to open up and thereby stimulating the player economy.

Having access to measurable economic indicators is an important part of good economic management, and the introduction of guild shops could be a good step in this direction for Bliz. Not to mention the fact that setting up a guild shop and selling stuff through it should be pretty fun, and a good way to raise income for your guild.

l)ragonSword, do you think having a fixed amount of gold per server could help solve any problems? Lets say that a particular server has, to pick an arbitrary number, 1,000,000,000,000 gold on it. This would mean that no more than one trillion gold can be found on that server. This one trillion gold has to be accounted for in both the amount of money all the players on a given server possess and the total amount that is in the economy (shops and every single creep camp on the entire world) Since the amount of money would be fixed, Blizzard could then use some form of price control to bring the economy into equillibrium. For instance, lets say that Blizzard finds that on one server there is a surplus of a certain kind of item(s), or not a lot of people are spending money. Blizzard could then lower prices (which would include cost of items, skill trainers, and money gained by creeping) to entice people to buy more and put money back into the economy so that other players can gain it some way (creeping, selling items, etc.) and spend it again. Also, if there was a shortage of a certain kind of item(s), or too many people were spending money, Blizzard could raise prices to prevent this mass spending. When it comes to people or guilds hoarding money, Blizzard could probably give GM's the ability to send thieves to steal money and put it back into the economy (kind of like Robin Hood - steal from the rich and give to the needy) I believe that having a constant amount of money per server will make it easier for Blizzard to control the economy and make sure gold doesn't become too scarce and valuable, or too abundant and worthless.

P.S. - A formula to describe the conditions in which the economy would be in equillibrium would be: Amount of gold all players possess (including money in banks) = Amount of gold all shops and creep camps possess
As money flows into the economy (shops, trainers, etc.) via players spending or trading, money flows back to the players via creep camps, selling items, trading. This keeps it all in balance in combination with Blizzard's price controls.
