To put it simply, you can't travel back in time, but looking at say a star millions of light years away is like looking back in time. You can however, "travel forward" in time. The faster you move in relation to the speed of light, the slower time moves. It's hard to envision but basically your body functions, a computer's clock, it all slows down the faster you go. If you took a rocket ship around the galaxy fast enough, you could arrive back at Earth during your lifetime, but everyone know would be long gone. Your lifetime would have increased because of the speed at which you were traveling.

Uhh, it doesn't matter what would happen if you go faster than the speed of light, because it's impossible. Now, I'm not saying the speed of light can't change, so it may be possible to go faster than 300,000 meters per second, but if you were doing that, the speed of light would have to be greater.

The speed of light is only the maximum speed we can percieve from Earth. There is no way an observer from Earth could ever measure anything as going faster than C. Even if your spaceship had a speedometer that said you were going the speed of light, an Earthbound observer would see you as going some fraction of it. As your speedometer approached infinity, speed relative to Earth would approach C.

So in a sense, you can travel as fast as you damn well please, but there is no way to go back in time, because to do so relative to Earth, you'd have to be going faster than the maximum observable speed on Earth, which means your speedometer would have to be above infinity. We all know that doesn't even make sense.

20. Re: OT: a question about time travel... | 1/26/2004 1:27:06 AM GMTST
First I'd like to say I know about the theory of relativity. What I'm going to state below may seem in conctradiction to it, but it seems it's actually not.

As stated in the book 'Time travel in Einstein's universe' by J. Richard Gott III., it could (in theory, of course) be possible to travel in time by finding a way to reach some place faster than the speed of light. That is impossible by a direct approach. But the book claims that, since gravity can bend light, it could be possible for one ray of light to reach a given location before another one, if one travels near an extremely heavy object and thus makes a slight curve (e.g. a black hole ... without the curve 2 rays of light could never reach the same destination of course). If you could travel, say, at a speed of 99,9% of speed of light on the trajectory of the 'faster' ray, you would reach the object before the second ray of light (the one that took a slight turn). Relatively to the second ray of light, you would be travelling in time (as you probably know, time at light speed is constant - it does not flow at all). And it would actually even avoid a singularity ('fatal error' in the fabric of space)

It's rather complicated, but this is the main idea as I understand it from the book.



Oh, and about the posts that by viewing light rays from far away you actually see the past ... that's NOT correct. The problem of that tought is that when you say that you view the past, you are using 'non-relative, ever-present time for the whole universe', but it was proven that there is no such thing. So you may view the state of distant galaxies that resemble the state of our own galaxy millenia ago, but saying that you see the past is not correct, because you can never view the past of our galaxy in such a way. It's just a sentence often used because it sounds interesting.
