121. Re: A Role-Player's Plea [Or Several] | 1/17/2004 7:33:54 PM GMTST
I would most certainly sacrifice a great deal in order to make in impression -- not necessarily to be a "hero", per se, but simply to contribute. I can think of a great many times I might have used that ability in Dark Age of Camelot; primarily to prevent my raid from wiping out. I believe that in a good raid, it should be the leader that sacrifices the most for the success of the mission, and I'm certainly willing to go that extra mile myself.

The effect should be related to the level of sacrifice involved, of course. If someone is willing to permanently lose a capped character, that's something worthy of a great deal of fanfare, and it should at very least change the course of an encounter or event, in my opinion. On a smaller scale, however, there still should be various different levels of sacrifice possible -- from the simple act of using a instant heal or the like to get a great deal of aggro and allow the party to flee, to the stated example of something that reduces your character to level 1 (the effective equavalavent of permadeath, really).

In essence, I simply think that accomplishment through sacrifice should be an option, where it has not truly been one in any MMORPG I know of to this date. There will always be other forms of achievement as well; in Shadowbane, I was named one of the first "Legends of Aerynth" for role-playing and leading events. Every game has its own standards for what makes a person great, and I'd like to see role-playing and sacrifice on the list for World of Warcraft.

I also would like to see as much versatility in raid tactics as possible, and I'd like to emphasize that important monsters should have quests tied to them whenever possible. In DAoC, the vast majority of the high-end epic mobs (Apocalypse, Beliathan, Xanxicar, Golestandt, etc.) did not have any quests associated with them. This is true of many Epic Mobs in Everquest and other games as well. I feel that having a quest to deal with them greatly enriches the role-playing experience, because it gives us an opporotunity to learn about the monster, as well as the feeling of impending risk. After all, Apocalypse is pretty impressive, but why hunt them down if they simply sit in Caer Sidi all day and aren't any threat to the world?
-Salaris, the Arcane Paladin

126. Re: A Role-Player's Plea [Or Several] | 1/18/2004 3:23:22 AM GMTST
Imperial, I don't think we have the capactity to gague how much role-playing we'll see in WoW until after Blizzard makes some sort of statement about their intent. Hopefully that will be soon, and then we'll be able to make some basic judgements on how things might be.

C'mon, people, need more feedback and comments on this thread.

Another point of interest for role-players is "Signature" items. I can't speak for everyone, but in my experience, most players I've worked with give each of their characters some sort of signature item when possible. This is often a weapon, a piece of jewelery, or an accessory of some kind in my experience -- it's rarely a full suit of armor or the like. In any case, one way to encourage this sort of thing is to allow for a system of upgrading items. This could be done similar to the "gems" system in Diablo II, or perhaps there could be a Quest to reforge any item at a higher level.

This would help alleviate "New Expansion Syndrome" for drops as well, since it's more likely people would put older items to use if they could somehow be increased in effectiveness. Another option would be having certain rare items that actually increase in level with the user; this would give a player a strong motivation to keep the item around. Items that gain experience points based on specific types of actions -- I.E. a sword that increases in power when the user slays demons -- also could be very interesting and viable options.
