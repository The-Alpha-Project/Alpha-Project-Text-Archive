1. Warfare Idea (Long) | 5/26/2003 4:19:57 AM GMTDT
A friend of mine and I have come up with an interesting warfare system that keeps the game 10% PvP, allows city siege, keeps the "war" feel of the game, encourages non-combative players and an ecomony, and adds a layer of strategy.

First, some terminology:

Fort: When we say "fort" we don't mean necessarily a true "fort". It could mean a small city, a tent encampment, a shipyard, whatever.

Control Points: A glowing circle on the ground that different factions can take control of.

Also, all numbers are subject to change however you deem fit.

The way this would work is theres a relatively large area of terrain, located between two "forts", one for each faction. The Forts are GENERALLY military cities each, surrounded by a wall, with NPC Archers defending them. The area between the forts is whatever the terrain of the area is, whether its rough barren plains, a forest, a coastal plain, or whatever. Now, the area has roughly 12 Command Points. Two within each fort (4), and 8 in the open areas between both forts. These Command Points spawn NPC Fighters for whichever side holds the point. A point is taken when someone of a faction stands on the point for a certain amount of time.

This battlefield set up allows more of the "hero" feel that you guys want for the game, with the PCs on each side kind of leading the NPC fighters into battle. Obviously, the NPCs be difficult to fight, but eventually, heros and small groups for each fraction could break through and gradually take over the points.

An idea that also came up for it is to add a cap to the number of soldiers that can be fighting at one time, so as to prevent one side from being to grossly overpowered than the other. For example, saying the cap is 200, 150 Human players are at the battlefield while 50 Orc players are there. This is obviously one sided in the humans favor. The way you even it out a bit is with the cap at 200, the Alliance side can only get an additional 50 troops in it (NPC Fighters), while the Horde side will keep spawning their fighters to a max of 150 NPC fighters. This way its overall more fair, and allows the Horde an actual chance against this. Adding NPC and PC players in the battle to meet a fixed number really helps keep this at least SOMEWHAT even. Again, all numbers are subject to change.

Each Fort is essentially a small military town, and people who do not want to participate in the battle(such as crafters) could congregate there and do business with the large number of PC fighters going in and out of battle, by selling them weapons, healing items, armor, etc.

Now, what benefit does this give for the PCs? Well, they can gain experience through killing the NPC fighters at each warzone. Also, eventually one side WILL win. You can set it up so that the winning side gains some sort of reward, such as gold, or maybe specific warfare related skills and abilities, or items that are related to warfare. Also, maybe gold depending on how much was done by a player in the battle. So if one PC just runs in, kills one NPC, and leaves, he'll get SOME minor reward for participating (We'll say 20 silver) but he won't get a major reward like the person who took 4 siege points and killed 100 NPC fighters and 50 PCs(Who might get 200 gold).

This also gives a good Roleplaying device for Blizzad to utilize. The winners at these battles can have an effect on battles to come. As the game uses instancing, we're assuming that when a battle is over, whichever side won "won" the battle, and you can just pick up the battle zone, place it somewhere else, and use a different town there. An example would be: The Horde manages to beat an Alliance group on the Plains of Kalimdor and secures the south side of the continent. Now, the next battlefield might be on the shores of Azeroth, with the Orcs have a temporary, tent-city there as a fort, while the Alliance has an actual fortification and the siege points and all move over.

This also adds a strategic and tactical feel to the game. Strategy could be very important in battle, as to protect your own control points, capture others, and depending on the set up of that particular battlefield, holding choke points like bridges, canyons, and your "fort".

There can also be a quest device within it if you so choose. Like, each fort can have a "commander" that offers PCs quests or mini-quests within the battle, like "Take and hold control point X within 5 minutes" or "hold Control Point X for a certain amount of time.

It also gets players out to take a look at the world, instead of a problem that a lot of MMORPGs and MUDs have, which is people just sitting around the city nearest the best hunting, and rarely leaving. These battlefields fit RPing well because people WANT to RP warriors in the Alliance or in the Horde. They can get involved with the major conflicts without having over 10% PvP (and hence can stop griefing since its implied you're willing to die and kill other players if you're in the battlefield)

People who don't want to fight also have an option here in this versatile system. They don't have to go to the battle field areas (wherever it may move on to the new "hot spot") and can sit back with their friends and do what they normally want to do, without fear of being killed.

Another positive aspect in tihs is that its relatively low maintenence. Once the base programming is done, it can simply be replicated onto additional servers, or be slightly modified for certain situations.

It also adds a whole new storyline and history depending on the servers. On one server, the Orcs might be dominating these battles. This changes the story of the world a bit as the orcs are holding the advantage in one server. On another, maybe the humans are dominating.

Third party battles can also take place with a battle field option, with maybe a tribe of Furbolgs attacking an Orcish settlement. An entire NPC invasion of a PC faction. The command points would be where their NPCs spawned, and either they gain special, more intelligent NPCs at their fort that try and take command points, or the GMs set it up in a convenient way so as to actually work. It also might be able to go three ways, or there might be interferring parties in a battle. Example: A human and an orc force are fighting each other. Its a pretty even battle and has been going on for some time, near the Forests of Kalimdor. All of the sudden, a bunch of angry Furbolgs come from the forest and begin attacking both sides.

Non-Warriors can also take advantage of the ritual casting system, and the buffs to their army so as to make a more dramatic and more strategic battle, as well as casting their offensive spells.

Anyone, what does everyone think? Comments? Suggestions? Flames?
