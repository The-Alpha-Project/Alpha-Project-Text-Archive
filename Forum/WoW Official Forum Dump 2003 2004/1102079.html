286. Re: A Role-Player's Plea [Or Several] | 1/29/2004 11:59:54 AM GMTST
Well, I can't really go into specifics on that. Not so much because of contractual issues but more so as professional courtesy to White Wolf as its still White Wolf's intellectual property.

My primary focus was all on the lore side of the fence. I pretty much worked with other authors, counseling them regarding the lore. The Codex was used as a foundation for alot of the work we did.

While I really can't get into the details, I can tell you that what I did was written in such a way that it promoted imaginative thought. I remember Stuart Wieck and Owen Stephens really loved the creativity level of it all. Last time I checked the EQ RPG site, all the stuff there was my work, the slogan "It's your world now!" was my creation when I finished off the original lore piece detailing the history of Norrath in the PHB.

Pretty much whenever I do any works, I focus heavily on promoting thinking. When I'm done writing I want people to be taken on an adventure where they can visualize the points I present as best I can to put them in the moment of it all. Give them the feeling and depth of the situation, thus pulling them in.

I've found this sort of writing works equally best when dealing with gamers and non gamers alike. The important thing here, is while writing with imaginative flare, you have to be certain that all your creative points are with merit. Everything has to make sense, have logical reason behind it, and carry purpose to it.

299. Re: A Role-Player's Plea [Or Several] | 2/3/2004 1:44:34 AM GMTST
Well, perhaps we should discuss the pros and cons of having a role-playing server here, since that appears to be a burning issue. I'll put some pros and cons out, and everyone is free to contribute to the discussion.

Possible Pros for having a Role-Playing Server:
  • A single place for all role-players to gather.

  • A relatively undisturbed atmosphere, where it is potentially less likely to see certain types of behavior and names.

  • Hypothetically easier enforcement of rules, since the server can have specific guidelines that are different from those of other server.

  • An easy way to find other people to role-play with.

  • Additionally, this would also help Game Masters locate Role-Players, if they are needed for any reason. Also, it may attract Game Masters and Blizzard employees to play on this specific server, and thus become a part of the role-playing community.

  • If time for Game Master Events is severely limited, a Role-Playing server would be a natural choice for where events should occur and be appreciated. Moreover, designating this as a "Storyline" server could mean that those things that occur on this specific server would affect the actual storyline of World of Warcraft if the players handle identical events differently on different servers. For example, in Everquest, the "Sleeper" was only killed on one server, and they escaped on all other servers. If the one server that had slain the Sleeper was designated a "Role-Playing" or "Storyline" server, that event could be considered what "actually happened" for the progression of the storyline.

  • Role-Playing servers tend to attract generally more "mature" populations, even if they're not all role-players. This can lead to a more friendly, and more tolerable, environment for everyone.

  • If no "Official Role-Playing Server" is made, players will probably designate a "Unofficial" one anyway; there simply will probably not be as many role-players there, since there isn't a "Official" flag or special treatment. Note that this could be both a good thing and a bad thing; some players may perfer to have a role-playing server with no special rules, but rather just something the community designates on their own as a place to gather.

    Potential Cons for having a Role-Playing Server:

  • Possibility of griefer guilds attempting to target and "take over" the Role-Playing server, similar to the history of the Mourning Server on Shadowbane.

  • Disadvanatages for role-players who choose not to play on the Role-Playing server. (I.E. decreased benifits, lower chances of having events on all servers, and less of a role-playing friendly atmosphere)

  • Increased difficulty for the spreading of role-playing and encouragement of Role-Playing in all of World of Warcraft in general.

  • The use of "I was just role-playing a {x)" to justify griefing on a role-playing server. While this is possible anywhere, it is tied to the problem of people specifically targeting role-players with griefing activities. In essence, by placing the vast majority of role-players on a single server, it's making a "bullsye" for certain groups of griefers and anti-roleplayers.

  • The possibility of over-enforcement of role-playing rules. For example, on Guinevere, the largest role-playing server in Dark Age of Camelot, several players had their character names involentarily changed after what was probably an unreasonable period of time post character creation. Twilight, the first player to hit level 50 on the server, had their name changed after they hit level 50. Essentially, when anyone has the capability of reporting any name, and any CSR has the ability to change their name, many character names can be changed in ways that the player base would likely deem unreasonable or unnecessary.

    This type of thing can happen anywhere, but the severe naming guidelines specifically associated with most role-playing servers and the players who often accompany those servers tend to make this problem more severe. In some extreme cases, some people in DAoC were reported to participate in "name griefing" -- reporting a character name as inappropriate to CSRs in a simple attempt to spite the player, reasonable or not. Sadly, in some cases, this tactic was even successful. The CSRs cannot be blamed for this; the rules for the server were simply too open ended and unclear, and clearer rules for a server will still almost invariably contain loopholes for some level of exploitability.

  • While the concept of a single server that advances the storyline, as discussed above, may be appealing to role-players, it ultimately may be considered unfair. Role-Players off the server would then have no ability to affect the story of the game, and in essence, the actions of every player on every other server in the game would lack the ability to affect the world in the same way that those on the Role-Playing server would have the potential to do. This kind of unbalance may not only be unfair, but difficult to manage, since it could also cause severe continuity problems if previous events are simply ignored for the other servers if they happened differently on the role-playing server.

  • World of Warcraft is being billed as a Role-Playing game, and some players feel that it is wrong for Role-Players to be treated as something of a minority group or afterthought. Rather, an ideal would be that all servers would encourage Role-Playing activities as much as possible, hold events, etc. By excluding events, specific naming policies, etc. to a specific server, Role-Playing is effectively being limited (but by no means prohibited) elsewhere.

  • Specific rules associated with a "Official Role-Playing" server may only drive certain Role-Players away. When Fironia Vie opened in Everquest, it had very specific altered rules; the removal of the "No Drop" flag and implementation of a "Trivial Looting" feature. This meant that certain items that normally could not be traded became tradeable; a feature that is very easily and powerfully exploited by power-gamers.

    The "Trivial Loot" feature prevented people from looting monsters that were too low level for them to gain experience from in combat. This was to prevent players from "Farming" lower level monsters to "Twink" other characters by giving them equipment that normally would be beyond their ability to obtain, or simply to sell the lower level gear which they could easily obtain, and thus deprive lower level characters of a hunting area.

    While the concept of the "Trivial Loot" code was sound, it was implemented in a game that was not initially designed for the feature. This lead to problems with completing a great many quests -- some of which were very important to role-players -- which required players to kill and loot lower level monsters. So, the code was removed. The end result was that the removal of the "No Drop" flag remained, but the system that kept it in check was removed, thus making it the easiest server to gain powerful magical items, since anything could be (and was) easily traded and sold.

    The other major rule change on Fironia Vie was the removal of the "Common" tongue. This was seen by many as a great blessing for role-playing, but others interpreted it as nothing but a chore, since the majority of players gain skill in other languages not through role-playing experience, but simply by "spamming" text over and over for someone else to gradually gain skill points in its use. In essence, it simply added another form of "griding" to the game, since Everquest's language system was not initially designed with that form of communication in mind. So, this system could also be interpreted as a failure -- mainly because the Role-Playing server, like the Player vs. Player servers, was implemented as a 'niche server and an afterthought.

    The end result? The "features" designed for Role-Players ended up defeating role-playing in certain cases, and helping powergamers obtain items more easily. While I don't think Blizzard would make such mistakes, the example is important to keep in mind for a discssion of possibilities with the implementation of a role-playing server.

    These are just a few points that I've thrown together; I'm not going to state that I'm any kind of authority on the topic. These are just views that I've heard, and neither side is completely right or wrong in any case. Again, some of my perceptions here may well differ from the experiences of others. Make your own decision, and please, post your points and thoughts here.
