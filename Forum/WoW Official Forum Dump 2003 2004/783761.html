OK here is an idea to move gold from M2 and M3 to (M6 - M5). That is, move gold out of player bank accounts and out of storage chests in player housing and guild halls, and into Treasury. This idea should also help combat the hoarding of gold and encourage high end consumption.

I call it Luxury Cruises. Sound like fun? It will be. First Treasury will construct (with help from a few Game Masters) a number of luxury cruises. These cruises will be scheduled to leave certain ports at certain times in various locations around the world. There will need to be a really nice ship constructed for passengers to live on during the cruise.

The ship will be much nicer than ships usually used to travel across the sea, and will include lots of refreshments and serving staff. There will be a casino, elven dancing girls, maybe a swimming pool, an arena for combat etc. Whatever the characters on the cruise need to ensure they have a great time. Perhaps it will be arranged for pirates to attack the cruise boat from time to time for entertainment.

The cruise destination should be an island that is unreachable by any other boat. This means the island will be completely unexplored. The island may consist of various regions, possibly including a dungeon, and there will be lots of opportunities for parties questing on the island to fight powerful opponents, gain experience, skill points, talent points, special training, rare items etc.

OK all that sounds great, so how do I get on this cruise? Well, Treasury will do a search of all bank accounts and all locked chests in player housing and guild halls, and wherever they find a concentration of gold higher than the trigger amount (e.g. 10,000 gold) they will post a marketing leaflet to the relevant player (or guild master) advertising the cruise. Obviously the luxury cruise wont be cheap!

So now the richest players in the world are given the opportunity to give up some of their stored wealth in return for some really cool rewards. Not all of them will take the opportunity, but some of them will and this will get hoarded gold back into circulation - to the benefit of the general population of characters on the server. 67. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/25/2003 4:54:04 PM GMTST
Good thread... here's an idea:

Why doesn't Blizzard just make it so that higher level mobs don't drop loot that has direct monetary value? In other words, at a certain point in the mid-high level range at which players become skilled enough to begin producing their own goods, mobs will begin to drop items that aren't worth much (or anything at all) to NPC's, but which are useful for other players. Mobs will also cease dropping gold. This way, craftsmen will find it too tedious to chase after lower level mobs, and the only practical way to make large sums of money would be by selling their products to lower level players. (Who obtain their gold through tedious mob-hunting.) So yes, it will be a player-driven economy, but at the same time inflation won't occur simply because the only money flowing into the game is generated by lower level players, while higher level characters spend the massive amounts of money they earn on more ingredients(to make more money), better equipment, and whatever other luxuries are available at high levels.

In this model, lower level players and higher level players depend on each other for manufactured goods and gold, respectively. As the server fills up with more and more higher level players, the number of people willing to sew the shirts will become greater than the number of people willing to pick the cotton, and thus cotton, err.. gold, I mean, will become a scarcity. This will encourage price-competitiveness among merchants trying to get their goods sold, and those who cannot manufacture quality goods efficiently will have to go back to the tedious work of hunting lower level mobs for their money, which no one wants to do.

Imagine a noob, going to the store to sell his loot after one of his first mob-hunting expeditions. At the store he encounters a suave player-merchant dude hawking his goods. "Hey, I can sell you potions at 50% of NPC market price, and armor at 75% market price, and while you're at it, I'm willing to take those Troll Testicles off your hands for double what that NPC merchant will give you for them."

noob: "Uh.. ok." :(

player-merchant: "Alright, thanks! If you ever need to buy anything, just msg me. I specialize in potions."

And that's the way it'll go. ^_^

If you want to develop this idea further, the Highest level players, who are too busy to hunt mobs or go shopping for ingredients or even sell their goods, can in turn, hire mid-level characters to do their bidding. It wouldn't be worth it for a level 60 alchemist to sell potions directly to noobs, but if he sells them in bulk to a level 20 middle man, it may be more profitable for the level 20 guy to resell potions for a living than to go mob-squatting with the noobs. The player-merchant in the above example is probably a level 20, since he is dealing directly with a noob. The profits he makes from his exchanges with noobs will go towards buying mid-level items manufactured by perhaps the same level 60 guy who sold him the bulk-package of potions to get his business started. This would require some careful price-planning on blizzard's part, but it's certainly feasible. Blizzard could also make travel-distance a factor, in which the middle-men must journey from the resource-rich areas where high-levels manufacture their goods, and travel back to the crowded noob areas to resell. A very fun player-generated-quest system.

So it's like a pyramid scheme, in which the wealth is generated by the lower level working class and quickly exploited by the highest level players with good management skills and trade networks. Only then, will they be able to purchase the extremely expensive sword from the NPC merchant. (Or the mass quantities of extremely expensive special ingredients with which they will manufacture the sword for themselves.)

I)ragonSword, what do you think? Do you see any potential flaws? I've seen this kind of player-driven economy work very well in MUDs such as "Federation" (It was a very popular MUD on AOL 3.0).

Hmmm. I see your point about crafted items Artic_Slicer, but I feel that having a total amount of crafted items is inevitable. However, I think I have a really good solution that would make it so that a player would never run into that issue you described earlier while keeping the total number of gold constant and accounted for. Let's say that there are a total of one million different recipies for every kind of equipment. (when I say equipment I mean every kind player crafted item be it a sword, potion, etc.) This means that the total number of different recipies that can be found by a player, regardless of what kind of item it will make, is one million. Now, have you ever heard of the substition property in math? (If A=B then B can be substituted for A in a formula) This property could be applied to help keep inputs fixed and the number of gold constant even with crafted items. Here's how:

An Elixir of Lion's Strength requires one snakeroot, one silverleaf, and one empty vial. Now, lets say that the total value of all of these ingredients is 25 gold. This means that the final product, the Elixir of Lion's Strength, would have to be worth 25 gold. A simple formula could be: VTI = VFCI

Value of total ingredients = Value of final crafted item

Furthermore, this new Elixir would have the snakeroot, silverleaf, and vial in it (in it meaning Elixir of Lion's Strength = one snakeroot, one silverleaf, one vial) at all times until the potion was consumed or sold to an NPC vendor. If the potion was to be used or sold, all of the ingredients would go back into the environment or into NPC vendor inventories, depending on whose stock is lower at the time. So, temporarily, as long as one Elixir of Lion's Strength is in existence, then one snakeroot, one silverleaf, and one vial do not exist. Well, they do exist, but they are just in a new form, the Elixir. Furthermore, the money value of these ingreidents is still there and accounted for but is just taking a new form as well.

Phew, now that has been said, I will tell you my next point. Arctic_Slicer identified the possible problem of limited crafted items. This would certainly stink if you couldn't make the sword that you wanted to because the max amount has already been reached. So, the solution is as follows: Make it so that every player on a given server may have a quantity of a particular item that is proportional to the rarity, strength and level of the item and the ingredients necessary to craft that item. For example. the total amount of level 100 Psycho Doom Swords per player would be extremely low compared to the amount of level 1 Noob Potions per player. To be specific, there may end up being some items that are only one per server, others that are limited to one per every 10 players, some that are one per player, or some items so common that they are like 20 per player. Since the kind of items you can craft depends on your proficiency level, what kind of skill you specialize in, your class, and what kind of recipies you have, the odds of high level players not being able to craft a powerful item is very unlikely.
