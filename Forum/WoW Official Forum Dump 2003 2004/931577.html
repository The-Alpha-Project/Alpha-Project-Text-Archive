BR - First post on fixed inputs:

l)ragonSword, do you think having a fixed amount of gold per server could help solve any problems? Lets say that a particular server has, to pick an arbitrary number, 1,000,000,000,000 gold on it. This would mean that no more than one trillion gold can be found on that server. This one trillion gold has to be accounted for in both the amount of money all the players on a given server possess and the total amount that is in the economy (shops and every single creep camp on the entire world) Since the amount of money would be fixed, Blizzard could then use some form of price control to bring the economy into equillibrium. For instance, lets say that Blizzard finds that on one server there is a surplus of a certain kind of item(s), or not a lot of people are spending money. Blizzard could then lower prices (which would include cost of items, skill trainers, and money gained by creeping) to entice people to buy more and put money back into the economy so that other players can gain it some way (creeping, selling items, etc.) and spend it again. Also, if there was a shortage of a certain kind of item(s), or too many people were spending money, Blizzard could raise prices to prevent this mass spending. When it comes to people or guilds hoarding money, Blizzard could probably give GM's the ability to send thieves to steal money and put it back into the economy (kind of like Robin Hood - steal from the rich and give to the needy) I believe that having a constant amount of money per server will make it easier for Blizzard to control the economy and make sure gold doesn't become too scarce and valuable, or too abundant and worthless.

P.S. - A formula to describe the conditions in which the economy would be in equillibrium would be: Amount of gold all players possess (including money in banks) = Amount of gold all shops and creep camps possess
As money flows into the economy (shops, trainers, etc.) via players spending or trading, money flows back to the players via creep camps, selling items, trading. This keeps it all in balance in combination with Blizzard's price controls.

DS - M1, M2, M3:

“Do you think having a fixed amount of gold per server could help solve any problems?”

Yes maybe so, I’ve been thinking about this. The funny thing about money supply is its not as easy to define as you might think. In the real world economists recognise that there are a number of measurements of “supply of money” in an economy, all of which are useful. For example M1 might be money held in cash, M2 might be M1 plus money held in savings accounts, M3 might be M2 plus money held in term deposits and investment accounts, etc.

As you go up to M4 and beyond money becomes less “liquid”, less free to be exchanged and flow through the economy. So to manage the economy a government will always have an M1 target, an M2 target, an M3 target etc. each of which needs to be monitored and fine-tuned through economic management. So, in the WoW economy I guess M1 will be gold actually held by characters, ready to spend. M2 will be M1 plus gold deposited in player bank accounts.

M3 might be M2 plus gold held in chests and boxes in guild headquarters and player housing. M4 might be M3 plus gold held by monsters, shopkeepers and NPC characters below a certain rank. M5 might be M4 plus gold reserves held by the highest ranking NPCs like Kings, Warlords and Dragons. So Bliz could have a target for M1, M2, M3, M4 and M5 and each of these targets could be monitored and adjusted.

So by your suggestion to keep the total gold on the server “fixed” we are actually talking about fixing M5. This still leaves Bliz free to encourage movement of currency between M1, M2, M3 and M4 to manage inflation and unemployment. Now I really like it that you have suggested an economic formula, because economists love formulas. So I’m going to suggest some of my own formulas for the WoW economy...


M5 target can be one billion gold (or whatever amount Bliz chooses for the server total)

M4 target = (M5 – M3) x 80% + M3

M3 target = C x L x 100 gold

Where C = number of Characters active on the server
Where L = average Level of all characters on the server

M2 target = M3 x 80%

M1 target = M3 x 20%


To put the above into words, this means keep the total gold on the server fixed, let players have some part of that total based on how many characters there are on the server and what levels they are, and the rest is kept by NPCs. The top level NPCs will have 20% of total NPC wealth, the rest will be distributed among shopkeepers, monsters, will be guarded by creeps and NPC gangs etc. Then of the wealth that is held by players, make sure not more that 20% of it is hoarded in chests in guild headquarters and player housing, and not more than 60% of it is saved in the bank.

BR - Second Post fixed inputs:

Heavenlyrain, how do you know that NPC vendors will offer nothing and that the economy will be a barter system between players? I fear that you have excluded a very important factor: simple little goods that allow players to craft items (herbs, minerals, etc.) I have here a very simple counter example to your argument: Billy is a human mage specializing in alchemy and herbalism. He needs some herbs to make a potion. Billy could either look for the herbs himself, buy them from a vendor, or find a player who just happens to have the right amount and right kind of herbs and is willing to either give them, sell them, or trade them to Billy in exchange for something else. In my opinion, trying to find someone who has the right kind of herbs and is willing to give them to you is too tedious, especially when I could just find them myself or buy them! So for now, lets temporarily rule out trading for herbs with other players. Now, lets say that all of the inputs on this server (i.e. total amount of money and total number of players) are fixed. Most likely, if someone is looking for some herbs they are going to buy them from a vendor. However, what if it turns out that the demand for herbs is decreasing because players find that the prices are too high and the herb is abundant enough that they can just find it instead? A simple resolution to this problem would be for Blizzard to lower the price of the desired herb a little bit and make the herb less abundant in the wild. This would entice players to buy the herbs instead. This brings me to my next point/suggestion. I think that along with a fixed amount of gold and number of players per server, the number of items (such as hebrs) should be fixed as well. For example, lets say that the total number of a particular kind of herb (lets call it herb "x") is 100,000. The 100,000 plants of herb "x" must be accounted for in the wild and in the inventories of NPC vendors. This suggests the simple formula: HW + HI = TH.

HW = total herbs in the wild
HI = total herbs in NPC inventories
TH = total herbs on server

However, since this formula doesn't account for the number of herbs in player inventories, a similar M1, M2, M3 system could be put into place. Instead of M1 it would be H1 (where H = herbs rather than money). All in all, the variables might be something like this:

H4 would be the total amount of herbs
H3 would be the amount of herbs in the wild
H2 would be the amount of herbs in all NPC vendor inventories
H1 would be the amount of herbs in player inventories

Their relationships are very simplistic and would be as follows:

H1 + H2 + H3 = H4
H4 - (H2 + H1) = H3
H4 - (H3 + H1) = H2
H4 - H3 - H2 = H1

I believe that this system would work in combination with price controls on behalf of Blizzard, provided that every input was fixed. After all, economic models are so much easier and more fun when there are no varying inputs. It makes equillibrium much easier to achieve and helps eliminate inflation. Anyways, tell me what you think of this idea. :D

P.S. - When it comes to hoarding, I highly doubt that players will hoard herbs unless it is strictly out of spite to other players. However, if this is the case, GM's will most likely be able to intervene (They could be like the economy police!)

P.S.S. - Some of you may be asking the question: Where would the herbs go after they have been used by a player? I think that once an herb has been used it should either go back into the wild or into NPC vendor inventories. Depending on the number of herbs currently in H3 and the number of herbs in H2, the used herb would go back to the one with the least amount of herbs. [i.e. A player uses an herb. The number of herbs in the wild (H3) is less than the number of herbs in NPC vendor inventories (H2). The herb would then go back into the wild (H3).] This way there would always be enough herbs to account for H4.

Banana Rebellion



All right, I admit it, I am a Monetarist! I actually studied Monetary Economics at University. Monetary Economics is a school that focuses on Money Supply above all else. We formulate targets for M1, M2, M3 etc. and develop tools and mechanisms to move currency into (and out of) each of these. We believe that if money supply is handled properly, everything else will kind of take care of itself.

In the real world I think Monetary Economics is pretty nice, because it is very important to maintain the value of currency and prevent inflation and recession, monitor consumer spending patterns and wealth distribution. However, in the real world its fine if the value of non-currency wealth in the economy (that is "items") just keeps on increasing. I mean are citizens going to complain if one year they are all riding motor scooters, the next year they are driving hatchbacks, the year after that they are all in Mercedes, etc? No they will love it!

However in a game world I admit there will be a problem if there are so many good items around that everyone can easily get their hands on the best possible gear for their character. It will take away motivation to buy stuff from NPC vendors, leading to reduced flow of capital from the player economy to the Treasury. It will also detract from the value of performing a variety of activities that have items as their reward (like questing). My previous posts have not ignored this factor, but I focused on money supply first because that is the kind of economist I am. ;P

So, lets take a closer look at the value of items in the economy. First of all I want to make the point that in WoW every item that exists will have a (hidden) value assigned to it by Bliz. When an item is first introduced into the game world this (hidden) value will be a "rough guess" by Bliz related to how useful the item will be and how badly players will want it. If you want to know the hidden value of an item sell it to a NPC vendor then buy it back. Now add the sale price and the purchase price and divide by 2. This is (roughly) the hidden value, simple.

Over time Bliz should observe what sort of prices players are paying for this type of item in the player to player market and modify their initial hidden value to reflect real world value. Bliz will also be able to check how fast the item sells from NPC vendor inventories. If the item sells-out in shops really fast it is probably underpriced, if no one buys it then it’s probably overpriced, etc. So, my point is that because of these hidden values Bliz will be able to calculate at any given time the total value of items and equipment on a server.

I propose we take the approach that value of items and equipment on a server be tied to M6 (amount of currency on the server). Also total value of items held by players should be tied to M3 (the amount of currency held by players). I suggest that if it’s OK for a level 20 character to hold about 2000 gold (including in bank, in house, in guild account) then it should also be fine for that character to have about 8000 gold worth of equipment and items (some worn, some carried, some in storage).

We can easily produce some formulas backing up this proposition:

IET target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEK target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEV target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEC target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 40%
IES target = (M3 x 4) x 60%
IEW target = (M3 x 4) x 40%

Where IET = total value of Items and Equipment held in Treasury
Where IEK = total value of Items and Equipment held in the strongholds of Key NPCs (for quest rewards, used by Game Masters etc)
Where IEV = total value of Items and Equipment held by NPC Vendors in their inventories
Where IEC = total value of Items and Equipment held or guarded by Creeps, monsters and other NPCs ready to be dropped upon death
Where IES = total value of Items and Equipment held by players in Storage (in bank deposit boxes, in player housing etc)
Where IEW = total value of Items and Equipment actually Worn (or carried) by players

So, in the above model the total value of items on a server will be held (more or less) constant. This doesn’t mean the server will always contain the same items. It will be up to Treasury to remove items from the game word, introduce items to the game world and adjust (hidden) prices on items and equipment to ensure total item wealth on the server remains close to the target.

Items will flow to Treasury from purchases by NPC vendors, player death item drop penalties, character deletion (when a character is deleted from a server their items will go to Treasury), item use (drink a potion, eat a chicken, use some iron ore to make a shield, use some magical ingredients to cast a spell, etc.) and possibly also item decay.

Items will flow out of Treasury through spawning of creeps and monsters (who hold items ready to drop), distribution of items to the inventories of NPC vendors for sale, distribution of items to Strongholds of Key NPCs for use by Game Masters as quest rewards, gathering of resources from the landscape using neutral skills (herbs, food, wood, jewels, metal) and creation of items through trade skills, item rewards offered for Arena Combat and achievement in PvP Faction War areas, etc.

So the main problem is to keep the value of items flowing into Treasury roughly equal to the value of items flowing out of Treasury. We already discussed how it would be useful for NPC vendors to hold a certain amount of currency. So it will also be useful for NPC shopkeepers to keep a certain quantity of items in inventory for sale to players. Inventory target levels will be determined by what kind of shop it is, the size and location of the shop, number of players passing through the shop each week etc.

At the end of each week Treasury should clear all shop inventories across the server, take in any excess stock (resulting from items being purchased from players) and distribute new items to those vendors whose inventories are running low. Again if certain vendors are constantly selling out of certain items Treasury should look into why this is occurring. Are these items seriously underpriced for that part of the world? Or, if there is a good explanation, inventory target levels for the Vendor may need to be adjusted.

If a NPC vendor finds they are running low on an item they should automatically raise the price for that item until they receive their next shipment from Treasury, and continue raising their price until players stop buying it or they sell out. This will prevent flooding of underpriced items into the game world before Bliz can catch it.

Also if Game Masters in some parts of the world are being too generous in distributing items from local Key NPC Strongholds (as rewards for Questing, or Arena Combat, or PvP Faction War achievements etc.) then Treasury will see that refilling those Strongholds each week is becoming too expensive. So they can talk with local GMs about this and take corrective action.

Still, even with these suggested checks and balances I can see that some posters will still be concerned that flow of items into the game world will outweigh flows out of the game world.
