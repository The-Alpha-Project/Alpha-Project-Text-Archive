i've played extensively on the hardcore PVP servers for UO (seige perilous), DAOC (Andred) and tried the KVK server for AC2 (Coldeve). the biggest problem i found with unregulated hardcore PVP servers is that inevitably one side or one alliance or one guild will completely dominate and decimate their enemies in almost all PVP encounters due to vast superiority of numbers.

this happened over and over and over on Andred and eventually killed the server. guilds who couldnt hack being on the receiving end of that uber guild (which always had every relic) either disbanded with members quitting, or switched sides leading gradually to a more and more boring server state. the dominant alliance had almost no one to kill. the enemies of that alliance got so completely annihilated without any chance of fair and even PVP that they quit, left or switched sides. one might think that some sort of competition equilibrium would be reached, but it's my opinion that human nature dictates otherwise. more people flock to the winning side for the sake of saftey and superiority than vice versa for the sake of challenge. look at the situation with the Lakers picking up Malone and Payton on bare minimum salaries. most of the time the NBA regulates competition with the draft picks, but free agents can sometimes throw things out of whack, and then true human nature reveals itself. do Malone and Payton want a challenge? do they want 10+ million bucks a year vs 1.5 million? no, they want to be on the winning team at all costs, even if that team is almost a joke of unfairness.

hence, when AC2 came out i decided to try their KVK server in lieu of the hardcore PVP server, it was my hope that it would have most of the open PVP benefits of a hardcore server, yet retain some balance between opposing sides due to the kingdom restrictions. boy was i wrong. when it seemed like Dominion guilds had the slight edge at the beginning, everyone started changing factions and going dominion. then a few of the top dominion guilds got bored and decided to switch to shadow and started kicking butt. sure enough, 75 percent of the server became shadow. needless to say, Order's turn was just around the corner.

i'm sure there are servers and games out there that have good systems setup for maintaining opposing side equilibrium. i just haven't seen them yet. if WoW does create a hardcore PVP server, i hope this is one of the things they will address.

35. Re: One server for PvP anywhere | 7/16/2003 8:23:31 AM GMTDT
I came up playing a Mud called Duris... It was based on RvR Goodies: humans, elves, gnomes, halflings, etc. vs. Evils: Orcs, Ogres, Trolls, Drow, etc. This was a VERY intense and addictive game. Full PvP was allowed (the exception was in major goodie cities where guards would come out and arrest your character if you were to engage in pvp in town). Evils were full PvP even in their cities.

What ended up happening was guilds began to self regulate the atmosphere... You would have your random pk's from time to time as a goodie by another goodie, however, for the most part you were watching your back for raiders. Guilds became a regulating force. They would protect their comrades from being pk'd by other goodies and would raid evils together. Guilds would band together to form alliances and sometimes would fall out between one another and go to war. You couldn't imagine the politics behind it.

On the evil side they really picked up the mentality of might makes right. There was essentially only one evil guild which was VERY strong. They would PK the hell out of anyone leveling up to the point where you would almost think it was not worth it. But then once you made it to a certain point... or proved yourself to that guild you gained respect and the potential to belong to the guild.

It was so intense the evil side even got one really kickass leader, a Duergar assassin, who basically could lay bounties on people etc.

I think the reason this style of PvP was so successful was because the world compared to the gaming population was enormous... So you could get a group together and explore a new zone for xp and be realitively hidden from the other side (but you would always keep one finger ready to book it in case a pvp group showed up)... Some of my best experiences on that game were getting whacked but putting up a hell of a fight.

Oh and to make it more interesting it was full loot as well...

40. Re: One server for PvP anywhere | 7/17/2003 12:02:59 AM GMTDT
I disagree that adding special servers is just a way around an issue when the developer can't meet the needs.

Some people want little to no rules. They want to PK and loot whenever and wherever they want. It's hard to develop that into a "normal" server. If the demand is high enough, then I think an open PvP server should be created. You're not going to appease the hardcore PvPer if you limit when or where they can kill -- but a limit to PvP is how MOST people want to play.

You can't make a server with no rules and rules at the same time. An open PvP server doesn't hide deficiencies in an MMORPG; it's more like a different flavor of the same game (or like anchovies on a pizza -- most people don't like them, but those that do, swear by them... Hehe if PvEers are "carebears", then hardcore PvPers are "anchovies").

The problem is that there are usually not a large amount of players that want to play on these servers... take away the griefing of non-PvPers and you see who the REAL hardcore players are -- they play on those servers.

As for PvE servers, they probably wouldn't be full either -- its just the other extreme type of play. Most people, I'd guess, are in the middle where there are PvE and PvP areas.

I've played DaoC and Shadowbane. I like DaoC's RvR system (other than the time needed to get to a competitive level) because I can choose when to do it. I know the risks of being in a PvP area, and I can relax outside that area.
In Shadowbane, the normal server rules were definetely much more close to open PvP, and griefing existed almost exclusevely in the form of stealthers, though in most cases other classes were hesitant to randomly kill because they feared what it would do to their reputation. However, the environment got old quickly -- stealing, looting, backstabbing on a constant basis. These are griefers' basic tools and often came from someone 20 levels above you. My point? The open environment eventually burned me out because I did not have the time to invest to keep up with the hardcore PvPers -- they were always two steps ahead. I went back to DaoC where I have a chance to relax once in a while.

For me, MMPORPGs are a game... for others, it's a way of life. Those two philosophies don't mix. Thus the support of a separate server.
