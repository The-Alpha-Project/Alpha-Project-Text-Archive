I like a game that allows players to try a wide variety of things, to make their characters look unique, and that gives good reasons to hunt, craft and socialize without forcing the player to do any of them.

I dislike a game that bores me with a long, pointless level or skill grind, that allows players to have unfair advantages over other characters of similar class/level, or that doesn't have a point beyond levelling and/or pointless PvP.

MMOs that I have liked best were:

DAoC - lots of little annoyances were cleared up - you didn't have to have a certain character class bind you, you didn't have to do corpse runs, and there were lots of interesting areas to explore. The classes tended to get a bit dull, though, because they had essentially the same set of abilities from level 10 through 50 (they just got better at them) and every spell and every power was geared towards combat or combat support (there were no "fun" spells or activities, like fishing or getting drunk).

SWG - Huge areas to explore, wide variety of things to do. The first game that ever gave us a reason to hang out in the tavern! What ruined SWG for me was the little annoyances that SOE can't seem to get past - they insisted on keeping bizarre game mechanics like Pokemon-style pets, vehicles that made you unkillable in dangerous areas, critical failures on crafting, and holocrons which encouraged players to macro-grind professions while AFK. On the bright side, SWG catered to solo players and casual players very nicely with lots of great places to hunt or craft, and ways to make items or gather resources while offline.

Ok attempting to re-write what I had (in Word this time hehe).

What I liked most:

1) Dragonrealms skill system. Ok basically it was a skill based game with no skill caps. You gained experience from using skills. When you had enough experience your skill went up a rank. Each successive rank got more and more difficult to attain. You had to do more challenging things in order to raise it. For instance if you had 100 ranks in your weapon then you couldn’t gain any experience in it from fighting level 1 monsters, you had to fight something that was challenging for you. A low level skill might take 5 minutes to gain a rank while a high level skill takes up to 6 or 7 hours of constantly working on it. But it was fun to work on skills, it wasn’t a chore. There were about 60 different skills in the game.

2) Dragonrealm’s level system. You leveled up within your profession after you gained so many ranks in specific skills. Your trainer would tell you exactly what skills you needed to raise. When you leveled up you gained points to spend on your attributes. These attributes are skill modifiers. For example someone with 100 ranks in their weapon and 50 strength would hit harder than someone with 100 ranks in their weapon and only 30 strength. There was no level cap, but just as the skill system it got more and more difficult as you got higher level. When you’re really high level you might level up once every 6 months. But you’re constantly gaining skills so your character is constantly improving even if you don’t level. It’s a skill-based game.

3) Everquest’s monsters. I loved how they had all different varieties of challenge levels for their monster encounters. Some of them took 100 high level people to kill, some took 20, some could be solo’d. At any level you could find the perfect monsters for you to kill. At any level you could join a group and find the perfect monsters for that group to kill. And because you couldn’t kill everything you could always look forward to killing those higher level monsters. There were endless goals in the game.

4) DAoC’s PvP system. I loved how the 3 realms were separated and you couldn’t even talk to your enemies. No smack talking, just a lot of fun and challenging killing. The frontiers were great to exp in because you could run into an enemy player any time. They truly gave you a sense of danger. I loved the keep-taking as well. Only drawback was it was difficult to help out if you weren’t high level.

5) DAoC’s death system. Not too harsh, not too light. When you died you respawned with all your items. You would have resurrection sickness, which halves your damage for about 10 minutes. You lost some exp as well. The first death of any level you lost very little experience. The second death of that level you lost a little bit more, and a little bit more with the third death. Any death after that is the same. The experience loss wasn’t that bad, maybe 10-30 minutes of time lost. And you could go back to where you died and get half the exp you lost back, but you didn’t have to.

6) DAoC’s spec system. I loved that there were different ways to build your character even in the same class. For instance one Healer could spec to be great at healing his comrades, while another Healer could spec to be great at crowd control. They would both have vastly differing abilities but they still filled the same basic role. It made people more unique.

7) Everquest’s epic and other multi-step quests. Really fun to do. They were great for many reasons. You got a great item. There are so many steps it gave you something to do all the time and you got a huge sense of accomplishment after you completed each step. You needed your friends to help you. It really felt great when people were willing to help you and you could share your joy with them. Some of them were just plain really fun like the 10th ring quest.

What I liked least:

1) Everquest’s grouping/class balance. Maximum group size in Everquest was 6 people. Every group required a cleric (for healing and exp res), a warrior (for tanking), and an enchanter (for crowd control). These three classes made up about 25% of the population and 50% of every group. The other 75% of the population had to fight for the remaining 50% of the group. This meant that 25% of the population would be completely unable to find groups. They had to sit around and wait for other people to leave their group. Terrible system and they never bothered to try fixing it because then those three special classes would complain.

2) Everquest’s death penalty. Just terrible. If you died in the middle of nowhere you had to spend your entire night getting your stuff back. You had no choice, you can’t afford to lose all your items in that game. Then when you did get it back you had to spend the entire next night getting your experience back. The reason this was terrible is because at least half of my deaths were from losing connection, not from lack of skill at playing the game. When a whole raid died in a bad spot you all had to spend the rest of that day just getting back to your corpses and the raid is usually ruined.

3) SWG’s rushed release. Ugh. Horrifying bugs were still in the game. Some classes were still useless. There were exploits where people were duplicating stuff that ruined the economy. Chat system didn’t even work most of the time. Just a long, long list of things that weren’t ready for release.

4) SWG’s pet/combat system. Ok each character could have 5 pets and all of them were more powerful than the character himself. Completely unbalancing. Pets dominated PvP and PvE alike. With the help of their pets people were able to kill the toughest monster in the game all by themselves. Pets had way more life than characters did. This made them the only things capable of tanking a mob. There was very little crowd control in the game. Basically you could barely hunt if you didn’t use pets. This led to people calling the game Pet Wars instead of Star Wars and lots and lots of pokemon comparisons. IMO a character should only be able to have 1 pet and that pet should be less powerful than the character himself. It’s the only way it works.

So far I've played ACII, AO and FFXI

Asheron's Call II
Likes
  • Character graphics. Specifically, I loved the way women were modeled in that game.
  • Story integrated into quests (in a somewhat crude manner..)
  • Accessible interface. It was very easy to learn.
  • Good newbie initialization area.
    Dislikes
  • Complete lack of content!
  • Vacant cities.. it's okay when it's empty everywhere else, but when cities are empty it feels like the whole world is some ghost reflection of what it should be.
  • The 'spell tree'.. there wasn't enough of a variety for me to get excited about advancing in that tree. Each spell seemed to be almost identical to the last one in the same advancement path.

    Anarchy Online
    Likes
  • The level of character customization available (wearables, implants and nanos)
  • AMAZING COMMUNITY: have you SEEN their message boards? (now compare that to this message board.. )
  • Each class is very unique, with differences in tradeskills as well as combat.
  • The chat auction.
  • Being able to get items of value (at auction) at low levels without having to group. This happens when an item that can be used by a high leveler only drops to a certain low-range of levels.. admittedly there's farming going on as a result.. but people like me (consumerist players) NEED a way to make decent money at low levels.
  • Dyna camps - uber mobs in remote areas outside dungeons. They drop random uber loot. I had so much fun just hunting these down for a few days. No camping involved, because the drops are random. Great for solo players that need money, cuz they can take on a low-level uber mob and get decent loot they can sell.
  • ARKS - designated volunteer players that help out other players. My first time logging in I had an ARK greet me in person and I thought my eyes were going to pop. It was a pleasant surprise.
  • Shift + left click: practically everything in AO has a help text that can be called up in this manner.
  • The lottery effect: this is hard to explain but it's the greatest reason why I stayed in AO for so long. Because so many things are random (random quests with random loot, random drops off dynamobs, randomly generated items in shops, random player being nice and giving you stuff or hitting you with a buff.. cuz AO players are nice like that) there's always this feeling that something great might happen at any given time. It's an addictive feeling, and one I missed sorely when I switched to FFXI.
    Dislikes
  • I'm so sick of GREY. The new shadowlands is prettier, but it's also missing much of what makes AO great.
  • The random quest generating machine: all players in AO have a love-hate relationship with the quest machine. We love them because we can use clicksaver (a third-party loot sniffing program.. can't live without it) to roll items we need and quest for them. We hate them because it's easily all we do. The random quests are fun the first few times, then they quickly become routine, which is when you start blitzing them just for the loot.)
  • Lack of lore and fixed content. As a result of all that randomness, there's hardly a story in AO. Sure there's a PVP story with tower battle announcements once in a while, but I can't think of one quest I did that made me feel like I'm taking part in something greater.
  • Learning curve: it took me three 7-day trials for me to understand and finally sign up for the game. I think it's the implants that threw me off, but eventually it was the implants that kept me there. There must be a way to combine depth with easy access. I think all help files should have picture diagrams.
  • PVP tweaking: I'm sure PVP is great in AO, only if you're tweaked to the max. It's like some sick bourgeois version of PVP. You can take part in it only if your level 30 char has level 60 implants (equipped by crazy buffing) and uber tank gear that cost millions at auction. (At the same time, I love being able to tweak.. so I don't know if I'd give that up for this.)
