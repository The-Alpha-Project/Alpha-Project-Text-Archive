1. WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/6/2003 7:22:30 AM GMTST
Blizzard has announced that they will be managing the economy of the World of Warcraft very carefully, to maximise playability and ensure rewards and penalties are valued appropriately. When you think about it this should be pretty interesting stuff in practice, as the WoW economy will be fairly complex.

Diablo players will acknowledge that the Diablo economy is more or less stuffed. Players almost never bother to pick up gold from creature kills anymore because there's just no point. There is so much gold in the economy people will give you piles of it if you ask nicely. Players dont need to buy anything from shops other than scrolls and potions, and equipment repairs - all pretty cheap.

All the best equipment is obtained from monster kills or from player exchanges, so there is just no point in buying armour and weapons. Gold flowing into the economy through kill drops, sale of dropped items, new character creation and hacking far outweighs gold flowing out of the economy through player deaths and purchase of scrolls, potions and repairs. So currency (gold) has depreciated in value accordingly.

Now think about the WoW economy. Gold will flow into the economy through monster kill drops, bounties paid for mercenary work, quest rewards, mining, production and sale of items found or produced with neutral skills, etc. So to maintain the value of currency Blizzard will have to match these inflows of gold with currency outflows and keep the total gold in circulation fairly constant.

Gold will flow out of the economy through players purchasing weapons, armour, equipment, mounts, food, potions etc. from shops in towns. Players will pay gold to trainers to learn skills, pay for services like griffin taxi rides, ship transport, zepplin transport, banking, construction and furnishing of guild halls (and possibly player housing). There may be gold penalties for player death, possibly thieving by NPCs, quest related gold penalties etc. Gambling on fights might result in some gold outflow if the arena keeps a small cut of the proffits.

So, currency inflows and outflows will have to be monitered and controlled to ensure that the amount of gold (per capita) is more or less constant. But this is not the end of the challenge. Blizzard will also have to ensure that gold is circulating through the economy and not being hoarded by individuals or guilds. So Blizzard will need to put things (and services) in the world that cost a lot and are still worth buying to get the richest players to part with their hoarded gold.

Even this is not the end of the challenge. Blizzard will need to ensure that activities and items are valued appropriately, across all continents. For example if questing in one area produces far greater rewards than questing in another area characters will flow to that spot. Also if questing produces far greater rewards (for time spent) than mercenary work or use of neutral trade skills then no one will do mercenary work or produce stuff by hand for sale.

Similarly if some neutral skills produce far greater wealth rewards than others then there wont be a good balance of neutral skill learning. So I'm wondering how Bliz plans to track everything and keep the economic system in balance. Will there be one or more WoW staff that specialise in economic management? I'm guessing (and hoping) there might be. The last thing we want is a repeat of the Diabolo "gold is worthless" situation. :)

5. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/6/2003 8:54:20 AM GMTST
Hi, are you new to this thread? It’s pretty damn long isn’t it! Been going for a couple of months actually, which may be part of the reason. Anyway, so if you can’t read it all and want to skip to the end but still be able to understand what people are talking about, here are some economic formulas and stuff that I threw into the thread at various intervals that seem to have been absorbed into the discussion. Hope it helps! ><


Do you think having a fixed amount of gold per server could help solve any problems?

Yes, why not. In the WoW economy why don’t we let M1 be gold actually held by characters, ready to spend. M2 will be M1 plus gold deposited in player bank accounts. M3 will be M2 plus gold held in chests in guild headquarters and player housing. M4 will be M3 plus gold held by mobs, vendors and NPC characters below a certain rank. M5 will be M4 plus gold reserves held by the highest ranking NPCs (kept in strongholds and used as quest rewards etc).

So by suggesting that we to keep the total gold on the server “fixed” we are actually talking about fixing M5. This still leaves Bliz free to encourage movement of currency between M1, M2, M3 and M4 to manage inflation and unemployment. To put all this into economic formulas lets say:


M5 target can be ten million gold (or whatever amount Bliz chooses for the server total)

M4 target = (M5 – M3) x 80% + M3

M3 target = C x L x 10 gold

Where C = number of Characters active on the server
Where L = average Level of all characters on the server

M2 target = M3 x 80%

M1 target = M3 x 20%


In other words lets keep the total gold on the server fixed, let players have some part of that total based on how many characters there are on the server and what levels they are, and the rest is kept by NPCs. The top level NPCs will have 20% of total NPC wealth, the rest will be distributed among vendors, MOBs etc. Then of the wealth that is held by players, make sure not more that 20% of it is hoarded in chests in guild headquarters and player housing, and not more than 60% of it is saved in the bank.


Treasury and Super Macro Economic Optimiser

First the Treasury. This will be a pool of currency that will act as a kind of buffer, to give those in charge of managing the WoW economy a margin for error, and to cover economic cycles and unpredicted events in the WoW world. I propose that the amount of currency in the Treasury be 20% of the gold in the NPC sector. So we now need to introduce M6, which will be M5 plus Treasury. Macroeconomic formulas will be adjusted as follows:


M6 target can be ten million gold (or whatever amount Bliz chooses for the server total)

M5 target = (M6 – M3) x 80% + M3

M4 target = (M6 – M3) x 60% + M3

M3 target = C x L x 10 gold

Where C = number of Characters active on the server
Where L = average Level of all characters on the server

M2 target = M3 x 80%

M1 target = M3 x 20%


In WoW there will be some NPCs that take-in money and never need to give out money. I propose that since these NPCs don’t need to hold much gold, their income goes straight to treasury. NPCs that buy stuff and sell stuff on the other hand will need to hold some amount of gold. Each week Treasury will do an automatic clearance of all NPC vendors who hold gold. Those who are holding less than their target amount will receive gold from Treasury, those holding more than their target amount will pass-on the extra to Treasury.

Older purchased items held by the vendor will also go to Treasury. If a vendor buys heaps of stuff in one week and is running out of gold, then they will automatically offer lower prices when purchasing goods from players, until their (purchase) prices are so low no-one sells to them. This system (aside from working) should add some really useful economic indicators for Bliz. For example if certain vendors are constantly out of gold Bliz can check to see what is happening.

Mobs that hold wealth (ready to drop when they are killed) will be supplied with their gold directly from Treasury when they spawn. Gold flowing into and out of Treasury will be monitored, and Bliz will aim to keep these flows in balance so total gold in Treasury remains at about 20% of NPC held wealth. Now remember that the next 20% of NPC wealth will be held in the Strongholds of Key High Ranking NPCs.

I propose that when game masters construct quests they obtain the gold and items needed for quest rewards from the stronghold of the nearest Key NPC. Treasury will supply gold and item wealth to these Key NPC Strongholds each week, and each Stronghold will have a wealth target to be maintained. There may also be ways for game masters to move wealth from the local economy in their area to the local Key NPC Stronghold.


Now for the Super Macro-Economic Optimiser “O”. You will note that in our macro-economic formulas to-date the relationship between M3 (total wealth in the player economy) and M6 (total currency on the server) is more or less arbitrary, and depends on how many characters are on a server and how much effort they put into levelling-up, stuff that is kind of outside the control of Bliz. So I propose we form a link between M3 and M6 as follows.

Firstly we replace the 10 gold in the M3 formula with “O”.

M3 target = C x L x O

Where C = number of Characters active on the server
Where L = average Level of all characters on the server
Where O = super macroeconomic Optimiser

Now we define O as an amount of currency such that:

M6 x SN = Sum (M3st x BT) [for all servers]

Where M6 = the total amount of currency on each WoW server
Where SN = the total number of WoW servers operating
Where M3st = the M3 Target for each individual Server
Where BT = Blizzard [wealth distribution] Target (2) *

* BT will possibly start at about 20 at game release, and move towards 2 over a period of one year.

M6 will be assigned by Bliz (known), BT will be assigned by Bliz (known), number of servers will be known, therefore we do some simple substitution and replace M3st with the C x L x O formula. Bliz will obviously know how many Characters are on each server and what their average Level is. So the only unknown is O, which can now be calculated. O may end up being about 10 gold, or some other amount.

In plain English, what this means is that (assuming BT is 2): on average, across all WoW servers, the target for total gold held by Players will be 50% of the total gold on each server at any point in time. If at game release Bliz sets BT to 20 this will mean that the new (low level) characters flowing into the WoW world will initially only have access to about 5% of total server wealth. As Bliz moves BT from 20 towards 2 over time, they will make it possible for players to get hold of a greater percentage of the wealth on each server.

The final goal may be for the wealth held by players (on an average server) to be about 50% of the total wealth available (BT = 2). Some servers might have a higher population of characters, and the characters might be (on average) more leveled up, so on those server players might hold 60% of M6. On other servers with a lower, less experienced population players might hold 40% of M6. But across all servers, the Super Macro-Economic Optimiser will ensure that on average players hold about 50% of M6 (or another % if Bliz chooses).

The beauty of this method is it allows Bliz to directly control “O” through manipulation of BT, and therefore influence the value of the M3 target on every WoW server indirectly. You will note from our macroeconomic formulas above that targets for M1, M2, M4 and M5 are all directly affected by the M3 target, and therefore all monetary targets on all servers will move as a result of movement in the Super Macro-Economic Optimiser.


Item Wealth Targets

I propose we take the approach that value of items and equipment on a server be tied to M6 (amount of currency on the server). Also total value of items held by players should be tied to M3 (the amount of currency held by players). I suggest that if it’s OK for a level 20 character to hold about 200 gold (including gold in bank, in inventory and in player housing) then it should also be fine for that character to have about 800 gold worth of equipment and items (some worn, some carried, some in storage).

We can easily produce some formulas backing up this proposition:


IET target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEK target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEV target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 20%
IEC target = ((M6 – M3) x 4) x 40%
IES target = (M3 x 4) x 60%
IEW target = (M3 x 4) x 40%

Where IET = total value of Items and Equipment held in Treasury
Where IEK = total value of Items and Equipment held in the strongholds of Key NPCs (for quest rewards, used by Game Masters etc)
Where IEV = total value of Items and Equipment held by NPC Vendors in their inventories
Where IEC = total value of Items and Equipment held or guarded by Creeps, monsters and other NPCs ready to be dropped upon death
Where IES = total value of Items and Equipment held by players in Storage (in bank deposit boxs, in player housing etc)
Where IEW = total value of Items and Equipment actually Worn (or carried) by players


So, in the above model I’m suggesting that the total value of items on the server be held more or less constant. This doesn’t mean the server will always contain the same items. It will be up to Treasury and Game Masters to remove items from the game word, introduce items to the game world and adjust (hidden) prices on items and equipment to ensure actual item wealth on the server remains close to the target over time.


Bag Theory: Influencing the amount of item wealth in the player economy.

Obviously bags are going to play an important part in determining the quantity of items that players on a server will be able to store at any given time. A character will have 5 “bag slots” to equip with bags to carry items around in, and also an additional 5-10 bag slots in their bank account (depending on how rich the character is and whether they purchase additional slots) to store items in.

How will players get their hands on good quality bags? Some may come as quest rewards or MOB drops, but I suspect most good quality bags will come from NPC vendor purchases and from crafting. So, if actual player held item wealth moves above the (IES + IEW) target on a server, I suggest that the following events take place automatically to combat the problem in the short term.

Firstly NPC vendor prices for bags will increase across the server by a percentage. The percentage increase will depend on how far actual player held item wealth has moved above the target level. For example


PIWmod = (IEPc - (IES + IEW))/(IES + IEW) x 150

where PIWmod = Player held Item Wealth Modifier (percentage)
where (IES + IEW) = target for total player held item wealth (previously defined)
where IEPc = total Current value of Items and Equipment held by Players


So we can see that according to this formula, if total current item wealth in the player economy on a server was calculated at 110,000 gold and the target for player held item wealth on that server was 100,000 gold, then the Player Item Wealth modifier (PIWmod) would be 15% and all NPC vendor bag prices would rise by 15% across the server without any Bliz intervention.


This is not intended as a total solution to the item wealth problem. You will see further on in the thread that we have suggested that PIWmod feed into various formulas effecting things like high-level MOB drops and high-level crafter ingredient prices. OK, so I hope you found all that helpful, if a little crazy. If you want to skip to the end of the thread now you might find it a bit less confusing. ><

18. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 11/7/2003 3:15:09 AM GMTST

The trick is having it exit the world as fast as it's coming in... if you want to keep it valuable.


Cart before the horse. When a nation is printing currencies, they don't keep printing at a steady pace and then try to find ways to get it out of circulation. The key is not to get money out as fast as it's made, the key is to bring the money in as fast as it's leaving. The independent variable here is the rate of cash being "removed" from the economy, by "removed" we mean removed from players' inventory. If you try to constantly adjust prices across all of the continents in response to how much money players have, you'll end up with irritated players confused by NPCs charging prices that seem totally arbitrary and you'll still end up with players having massive amounts of cash lying about. It has to be pre-empted, the penultimate step is to gauge how quickly cash leaves, the ultimate step is to then adjust how quickly it returns (notionally).
